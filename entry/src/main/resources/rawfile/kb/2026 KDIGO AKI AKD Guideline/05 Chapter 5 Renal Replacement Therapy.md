# Chapter 5: Renal Replacement Therapy

5.1. Starting renal replacement therapy (RRT)
RRT is an important kidney and organ support intervention for patients with AKI in
modern clinical care. RRT may be required in a substantial proportion of acutely ill patients
with AKI, depending on the population-at-risk and clinical context (e.g., septic shock, liver
failure, cardiogenic shock).695 Recent trends show increasing utilization, particularly in high-
resource settings.696, 697 Decisions to start and discontinue RRT in acutely ill patients with
AKI are complex and influenced by a range of issues, includiTng overall prognosis (i.e.,
potential for survival, resolution of acute illness, and RRT independence), the relative
balance of risks to benefits, the availability of suitable expertise and resources, and patient
preferences (Figure 14).528

Practice Point 5.1.1: For children and adults with AKI, a process of informed shared
decision-making should be used with patients, family, caregivers, and healthcare

An essential consideration for treatment with RRT is the tailoring of decisions based
on the values and preferences of patients and/or surrogate decision makers (e.g., family,
caregiver) through an engaged process of informed, ethically acceptable, shared decision-
making.698 Shared decision-making can be defined as “a collaborative process that allows
patients, or their surrogates, and clinicians to make health care decisions together, taking into
account the best scientific evidence available, as well as the patient’s values, goals, and
preferences”.698
Shared decision-making is widely endorsed by numerous health care organizations,
including critical care and nephrology.698, 699 Patients/surrogate decision-makers report
greater satisfaction with care when confronted with transparent, consistent, and frequent
246

opportunities to communicate and share in the creation of the care plan with the healthcare
team.700 Shared decision-making can provide greater clarity on the direction of care.701, 702 In
selected circumstances, this may include foregoing RRT or escalations in care.703, 704
Provision of short-term RRT when benefits are uncertain may involve a time-limited-trial, to
build trust with the patient and/or surrogate decision-makers, strengthen prognostic certainty,
and shape consensus on decision-making for a care plan.705, 706 Time-limited trials can
facilitate a process of shared decision-making and have been shown to improve the quality of
family meetings and reduce resource use, including RRT.707
T
While RRT can be life-sustaining for patients suffering complications of AKI, access
to RRT services can be a challenge, particularly in low-resource settings. Barriers to RRT
include disparate hospital resources, geographic remoteness to a center with dialysis services,
and patient-incurred costs (i.e., inability to pay for any or a sustained period of dialysis).
F
Similarly, women are disproportionately less likely to access and receive RRT, especially in
low- and middle-resource settings.708, 709 In sub-Saharan Africa, mortality of children and
adults with AKI and indications for RRT but no access (i.e., did not receive) was reportedly
72%–79% and 86%, respectively, fAar in excess of the observed mortality of 24%–31% and
32%–31%% when RRT was available.709
There are numerous validated decision-making strategies (i.e., approaches; tools) that
are available and that can be adapted to facilitate complex decision-making around the
R
provision of RRT.698 The use of decision-making algorithms may improve consistency and
standardization of approaches and facilitate a framework for shared decision-making.154, 710
Key aspects of such a framework broadly include; i) clarifying available treatment options,
ii) weighing the relative risks and benefits, iii) understanding individual patient values and
D
preferences, and iv) articulating a schedule to engage in partnered decision-making with the
healthcare team.710
Recommendation 5.1.1: For adults with AKI, we recommend a strategy of deferred
timing of RRT initiation as preferred over a strategy of early (pre-emptive) RRT
initiation (1C).
This recommendation places a high value on avoiding the potentially unnecessary burden,
resource use and costs, and potential harms associated with earlier initiation of RRT. There
is low overall certainty of evidence of no differences in mortality, long-term RRT
dependency, or other serious harms with deferred versus early RRT initiation in adults with
AKI. A deferred RRT initiation strategy is based on the emergence of clinical indications for
RRT (medically refractory metabolic or volume-related complications of AKI), rather than
relying solely on creatinine or urine output-based criteria (Table 41). Deferred RRT
initiation requires the capacity for prompt RRT initiation when indicated, and healthcare
professionals should be mindful of local logistical factors when considering RRT timing.

Key information
Balance of benefits and harms
All forms of RRT are invasive organ support interventions that carry inherent risks,
which may increase with prolonged use. These complications can arise from the insertion and
ongoing maintenance of dialysis catheters (e.g., bloodstream infection, peritoneal infection),
therapy-specific adverse events (e.g., bleeding, hemodynamic instability, arrhythmias,
electrolyte abnormalities), and long-term adverse outcomes (e.g., delayed or nonresolution of
AKI).
T
The timing of starting RRT in the absence of conventional indications for RRT has
long presented a central dilemma for healthcare professionals (Table 41; Figure 14) and been
the focus of several RCTs in acutely ill adults.124, 128, 711-713 In these RCTs, acutely ill adults
with Stage 2–3 AKI and no conventional indications (e.g., metabolic, toxic or fluid-related
F
complications) were allocated to an early (“accelerated” or “pre-emptive”) or a deferred
(“standard” or “delayed”) strategy for starting RRT.
Deferred RRT initiation invAolves waiting until patients have medically refractory
complications or persistent Stage 2 or 3 AKI exceeding 48–72 hours. Most recent large
multicenter RCTs in adults have largely aligned around this definition of deferred RRT
initiation (alternatively termed delayed, late, or standard initiation),124, 712-714 however, there
has been some variation.128 This variation can present challenges when interpretating the
R
evidence across trials and applying it to individual patients. For example, of the 231
participants in the Early vs. Delayed Initiation of Renal Replacement Therapy in Critically Ill
Patients with Acute Kidney Injury (ELAIN) trial, 46.8% were enrolled after having
undergone cardiac surgery.128 AKI after cardiac surgery is common and may be associated
D
with a unique pathophysiology and constellation of complications when compared with other
groups of patients with AKI.715 This may include nuanced differences in the capacity to
tolerate complications related to AKI (e.g., fluid accumulation in the setting of reduced heart
function, electrolyte/metabolic abnormalities in a setting of increased risk of arrhythmias).
Systematic reviews have focused on synthesizing evidence on timing of initiation of RRT in
participants after cardiac surgery.716, 717 These have included relatively few RCTs128, 718, 719
and observational studies with high risk of bias.720-724 While pooled analysis implied there
may be benefit to earlier RRT initiation in participants after cardiac surgery with AKI, this
evidence has very-low to low certainty.

No differences in 90-day mortality, 90-day dialysis dependency, length of stay (ICU
and hospital), and serious adverse events were found between early versus deferred RRT
initiation (Supplementary Table S82).125, 726-728 This finding would imply that a deferred
strategy to starting RRT, characterized as a “watch and wait” strategy, where RRT is delayed
until confronted with worsening, persistent or medically refractory complications of AKI in
acutely ill patients, can be recommended as the default standard.
In a substantial proportion of those participants allocated to the strategy of deferred
RRT initiation across these RCTs, RRT was not started (range 10%–49%), due either to
kidney recovery or death, potentially contributing to lower resource use and costs of care.124,
128, 712, 713 However, in the Initiation of Dialysis Early Versus Delayed in the Intensive Care
Unit (IDEAL-ICU) trial, 17% of participants with septic shock allocated to the deferred RRT
249

initiation strategy did receive emergent RRT for medically refractory complications within
48 hours of randomization.124 This observation reinforces the need for regular reassessment
of the patients and also the further need for development and validation of clinical risk
prediction tools (e.g., diagnostic tests, dynamic assessments, etc.) to improve real-time
identification of patients most likely to derive benefit from starting RRT earlier.171, 180, 729
It is further plausible that accumulation and persistence of AKI-related complications
may exert the risk for adverse outcomes.730 In the Artificial Kidney Initiation for Kidney
Injury 2 (AKIKI-2) trial, acutely ill participants with persistent Stage 3 AKI without urgent
T
RRT indications were allocated to either start RRT immediately (aligned with the “delayed”
strategy in the Artificial Kidney Initiation in Kidney Injury [AKIKI] trial) or further deferral
(“more-delayed”), starting RRT only for a serum urea >50 mmol/l or an urgent conventional
indication.125 While there was no difference in the number of RRT-free days at 28 days
F
between the groups (12 days for delayed vs. 10 days for more-delayed, P=0.93), in a
prespecified analysis, 60-day mortality was higher in the more-delayed strategy compared to
the delayed-strategy (55% for more-delayed vs. 44% for delayed; adjusted HR: 1.65; 95%
CI: 1.09–2.50; P=0.07; SupplemenAtary Table S83).125 In addition, participants who were in a
coma at randomization had a lower chance of transitioning from coma to awakening and
remained comatose for longer.731 These findings support the concept that prolonged delays
should be avoided within a deferred RRT initiation strategy.
R
Individual studies provide further insights not captured in the formal syntheses. In the
AKIKI trial, the earlier RRT initiation strategy conferred a higher risk of catheter-related
bloodstream infections compared with a standard RRT initiation strategy (10% vs. 5%,
P=0.03).712 In the Standard (deferred) versus Accelerated (early) Initiation of Renal-
D
Replacement Therapy in Acute Kidney Injury (STARRT-AKI) trial, more people receiving
the earlier RRT initiation strategy experienced adverse events (23.0% vs. 16.5%, P<0.001),
including hemodynamic instability and electrolyte abnormalities (e.g., hypophosphatemia).
Finally, there may be important differences between specific groups of patients that
have not been the specific focus or have been underrepresented in RCTs (e.g.,
neonates/children732; cardiogenic shock733; solid-organ transplantation734, 735; extracorporeal
life support [ECLS]736), where there may be differences in the development of medically
refractory complications, differences in tolerating these complications, and/or differences in
relative indications for starting RRT and potential benefits with early or deferred initiation.
Certainty of evidence
The certainty of evidence in adults for the comparison of early versus standard (or
delayed) RRT is low.125, 726-728 The certainty of evidence was downgraded for imprecision
across outcomes, with inconsistency across studies pertaining to mortality and reporting
issues pertaining to adverse events, with sparse data for several outcomes.
250

One trial with methodological concerns provided very low certainty of evidence for
comparison of delayed (standard) versus more-delayed (very late) RRT.125, 726-728 The trial
had mostly very imprecise estimates of effect, but did find a significantly higher risk of death
with more-delayed RRT.
Values and preferences
Survivors of critical illness treated with RRT report impaired quality-of-life (QoL)
and function (e.g., new or worsened disability).737-740 This recommendation placed high value
on the potential for avoidance of RRT when applicable.
T
Resource use and costs
RRT represents an escalation in the complexity of care and is both resource intensive
and costly.741 In many low-resource settings, receipt of RRT is paid out-of-pocket, putting
F
patients at risk of accelerated economic decline. A deferred strategy for RRT initiation may
translate into a meaningful proportion of people with moderate-to-severe AKI averting use of
RRT and lead to concomitant reductions in resource use and costs.742
A
Considerations for implementation
The adoption of a deferred strategy for RRT initiation is likely suitable for most
acutely ill adults with moderate-to-severe AKI. However, variation in case-mix (e.g.,
cardiorenal syndrome, hepatorenal syndrome, post-cardiac surgery, concomitant organ
R
support with ECLS), and rapid changes in illness acuity and clinical status, and timeliness
and availability of essential resources (e.g., vascular access insertion, dialysis equipment,
staffing) may influence decision-making. Sound clinical judgement that considers these and
other local contextual factors is essential and requires frequent reassessment, especially in
D
rapidly evolving clinical scenarios. Adherence to guidance on RRT initiation can be
integrated into best practice advisories and monitored through quality improvement
methodology.743
Rationale
The decision to start RRT can be complex, given the need to balance perceived
benefits and risks, the escalation in resources (e.g., patient transfer, dialysis disposables,
human resources), and the importance of shared decision-making that integrates the values
and preferences of patients. A fundamental consideration for healthcare professionals when
considering whether to start RRT is to ensure clarity in defining the objective(s) and the
goal(s) of therapy. Initiation of RRT is appropriate for acutely ill patients with complications
attributed to or worsened by AKI, especially if refractory to medical therapy (e.g.,
hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, fluid overload), whose preferences and goals of care are
aligned with receipt of RRT. In the absence of urgent indications for RRT, the evidence
supports a strategy of deferred timing of RRT initiation over early RRT initiation.

Practice Point 5.2.1: The initial selection of RRT modality in children and adults with
AKI, including CRRT, intermittent RRT (IRRT), or acute peritoneal dialysis (PD),
should integrate patient characteristics, clinical context, local expertise, and resource
availability.
There is a spectrum of RRT modalities used to support acute and critically ill patients
with severe AKI who develop indications for RRT. These RRT modalities include
extracorporeal support with CRRT and IRRT as well as acute PD. RRT modalities are
characterized by differences in application (e.g., access, treatment schedules, dosing) and
T
mechanisms of solute clearance and ultrafiltration (Tables 42 and 43). There are variable
practices across clinical settings and geographic regions for the selection of RRT modality
for treatment of patients with AKI, due to differences in patient characteristics, clinician
expertise and preferences, institutional/organizational factors, and resource availability.
F
Further, patients may transition from one modality to another, for instance from CRRT to
IRRT. The ideal timing and clinical circumstances for RRT modality transition are uncertain
but are also influenced by patient characteristics and organizational factors.

Recommendation 5.2.1: For critically ill adults with AKI and hemodynamic instability,
we suggest using CRRT or acute PD over conventional IRRT (2C).
This recommendation places a high value on the advantages of greater hemodynamic
R
stability reported with continuous forms of RRT, including CRRT and acute PD, over
conventional intermittent RRT. The recommendation is focused on the selection of RRT
modality for patients with hemodynamic instability because it may exacerbate kidney damage
and impair or prolong resolution of AKI. It places a high value on the shorter duration of
D
RRT and ICU stay among patients treated with acute PD over conventional intermittent RRT.
This recommendation places a lower value on the greater resource use and costs of CRRT
compared with conventional intermittent RRT.
Key information
Balance of benefits and harms
CRRT, IRRT, and PD have specific advantages and potential complications.
Compared with IRRT, CRRT and PD enable more gradual ultrafiltration and solute removal
and may be particularly advantageous in patients with hemodynamic instability. (Table 43)
A large network meta-analysis found no difference in all-cause mortality, time on
RRT, recovery of kidney function, or length of hospital and ICU stay when comparing CRRT
with IRRT (Supplementary Table S84).764 However, studies included in these pooled
estimates comprised heterogeneous populations with varying levels of hemodynamic
instability (Supplementary Table S82). Moreover, many studies comparing CRRT with IRRT
excluded participants with hemodynamic instability because of the perceived risk of harm in
randomizing a hemodynamically unstable patient to IRRT. Although the available pooled
256

evidence suggested no differences in benefits or harms when comparing CRRT and IHD, the
majority of panel members perceived that the theoretical risk of harm with IRRT outweighed
the increased incremental cost with CRRT and, therefore, favored CRRT for critically ill
patients with hemodynamic instability.
The same network meta-analysis found that compared with IRRT, acute PD reduced
the length of time on RRT and ICU stay (with large apparent treatment effects), with no
difference in all-cause mortality or recovery of kidney function (Supplementary Table
S85).764 Collectively, this body of evidence suggests important potential benefits with no
T
apparent risk of harm, recognizing that acute PD may not be available in all care settings.
There have been few small, largely single center RCTs765-770 and systematic
reviews771, 772 that have compared the efficacy and outcomes of acute PD versus
F
extracorporeal RRT, including CRRT and IRRT, for critically ill participants with AKI.
Their findings have been largely supported by real-world evidence from observational
studies.773-775 One single center RCT compared acute PD to CRRT in 70 adults with sepsis-
associated AKI.766 While participanAts allocated to acute PD had worse outcomes, the
technique for acute PD was not aligned with current guideline-directed standards (e.g., use of
rigid catheters, use of manual exchanges and open drainage, acetate buffered solutions).776
More recent RCTs have suggested that acute PD can achieve similar and acceptable efficacy
and outcomes when compared with extracorporeal RRT; and PD may be associated with less

Certainty of evidence
The certainty of evidence among critically ill adults for selection of initial RRT
modality for the treatment of AKI is mostly low to moderate for comparisons of different
pairs of modalities. The comparison of CRRT versus IRRT has moderate certainty of
evidence,764 with downgrading mortality and recovery of kidney function due to
methodological concerns of the studies and downgrading of time on RRT due to imprecision.
The comparison of IHD and PD has low certainty of evidence,764, 765 with
downgrading of mortality and recovery of kidney function for methodological concerns and
T
sparseness of studies, but upgrading since network meta-analysis was concordant with the
findings of the single study; time on RRT was downgraded for a lack of direct comparisons
(conclusion was based on network meta-analysis), but with upgrading for a large effect size.
F

Practice Point: 5.2.2: CRRT is preferable to conventional IRRT for critically ill
children and adults with AKI and acute brain injury or other causes or risks of
increased intracranial pressure (ICP) or cerebral edema.
Patients with acute brain injury or cerebral edema may be susceptible to secondary

extend existing injury,792-794 especially in the penumbra surrounding the acute injury where
brain tissue may be recoverable. Patients treated with RRT, particularly IRRT, are
susceptible to intradialytic hypotension and rapid shifts in serum solute concentration and
osmolality.795 Treatment with IRRT in patients with acute brain injury can alter ICP and
provoke new or worsening cerebral edema, the degree of which correlates with the frequency

brain injury, particularly if there are contraindications to systemic anticoagulation. There is
limited evidence that has directly compared CRRT and PIRRT in patients with brain injury
or acute liver failure and the risk of disequilibrium syndrome and worsening neurological
profile. In circumstances where CRRT or acute PD is not feasible or available, the
T
prescription for RRT should be carefully adapted to minimize the risk of intradialytic
hypotension and rapid solute clearance associated with shifts in serum osmolality (Table 44).

small solute clearance. While earlier RCTs implied there may be a survival benefit with

5.4 Catheters for RRT
Practice Point 5.4.1: Vascular access for RRT, including type, size, length, and site of
catheter placement, should consider feasibility and weigh the risks of catheter
colonization/infection or bleeding with the desire to maintain catheter/circuit patency in
children and adults.
272

The selection of dialysis catheters for acute RRT in critically ill children and adults
should be guided by a balance between the feasibility of insertion, the risk of complications
(e.g., catheter colonization, infection, and bleeding), and the need to maintain circuit patency
and achieve adequate blood flow. In acute settings, nontunneled, uncuffed catheters are
recommended due to their ease of insertion, immediate usability, and suitability for short-
term use. This is particularly relevant in ICU settings, where rapid initiation of therapy is
often required. Cuffed tunneled catheters are not typically necessary unless prolonged RRT
access is anticipated or a transition to chronic maintenance dialysis is planned for patients
with AKI superimposed on advanced CKD.608, 820
T
The right internal jugular vein is preferred for catheter placement in both children and
adults.874 This site provides the most direct route to the superior vena cava and right atrium,
reducing the risk of catheter malfunction, kinking, and malposition.875 Additionally, the right
F
internal jugular vein is associated with lower rates of catheter-related bloodstream infection
and thrombosis compared to other sites.876

central venous stenosis, which can compromise future vascular access options. This is critical

Practice Point 5.4.3: Use of existing arteriovenous fistulas/grafts may be suitable for
IRRT in children and adults to avoid new dialysis catheter placement.
Practice Point 5.4.4: It is preferable to avoid using existing arteriovenous (AV)
fistulas/grafts for CRRT in children or adults, due to concern for needle dislodgement
or arteriovenous fistulae/graft damage with prolonged cannulation.
275

In the acute care setting, vascular access choice is a vital component of safe and
effective RRT. While existing AV fistulas or AV grafts are preferred for maintenance
hemodialysis due to their durability and lower risk of infection, their role in short term RRT,
particularly CRRT, is more nuanced. For IRRT in hemodynamically stable children and
adults, the use of a well-functioning, mature AV fistula/grafts may be appropriate. This
approach avoids the insertion of a temporary dialysis catheter, thereby reducing the risk of
catheter-associated complications such as infection, bleeding, thrombosis, and venous
stenosis.875, 901 This is especially relevant in patients who are already established on
maintenance hemodialysis or who have a functioning vascular access created in anticipation
T
of maintenance dialysis initiation.
In contrast, the routine use of AV fistula/grafts for CRRT is generally not
recommended due to several concerns. The prolonged cannulation necessary for CRRT poses
F
a significant risk of needle dislodgement, particularly in critically ill, sedated, or agitated

the anastomosis site, especially in nAewly created or maturing accesses. Unnikrishnan et al.
showed that venous needle placement during IHD dramatically increases turbulent shear near
the venous anastomosis, wall shear stress fluctuations increased from ~0.1 m/s to 0.4–0.7
m/s, 2 cm downstream of the needle tip, with turbulence intensities 5–6 times higher,
indicating this may contribute potential endothelial injury and intimal hyperplasia and lead to
R
AV graft complications.902 Existing AV fistula/grafts were not designed for extended

access for CRRT would require continuous bedside supervision.904 For these reasons, dialysis

Practice Point 5.4.5: The decision on how to replace poorly performing uncuffed
nontunneled dialysis catheters should be individualized. The options include a new
venipuncture or, in select cases, a guidewire exchange of the existing dialysis catheter.
The performance of uncuffed nontunneled dialysis catheters is vital for the effective
delivery of RRT. Inadequate blood flow rates or frequent circuit alarms can compromise
dialysis efficiency, lead to treatment interruptions, and increase the risk of thrombosis and
infection.905 When dysfunction is confirmed and cannot be corrected through flushing,
repositioning, or thrombolytic therapy, the catheter should be replaced. This may involve
either a new venipuncture at a different site, which is preferred when feasible to minimize the
risk of infection and mechanical complications, or a guidewire exchange of the existing
catheter, which can be considered in select circumstances, particularly when vascular access
is limited or when the patient is at high procedural risk.876, 906-908

Practice Point 5.4.6: Do not perfoArm routine or scheduled change of dialysis catheters
to reduce the risk of catheter-related infection.

catheter changes do not reduce infection rates compared to leaving catheters in place until no
R
longer needed or a complication arises.877, 910 Moreover, routine scheduled replacement
introduces additional procedural risks, including mechanical complications such as arterial
puncture, hematoma/bleeding, or pneumothorax, particularly in patients with coagulopathy;
loss of available vascular access sites, especially in patients with limited central venous
acces D s options; and greater procedural burden and healthcare costs.876, 909 These potential
harms are particularly significant in critically ill patients, who often have impaired
coagulation and require multiple concurrent therapies through central venous access.

Practice Point 5.4.7: In circumstances where prolonged indications for RRT are
anticipated, a cuffed tunneled catheter can be considered when initiating RRT for AKI,
particularly for children.

Non-tunneled, uncuffed catheters are not ideal for long-term use. In clinical situations
where prolonged RRT is anticipated or where preservation of central venous access is
particularly important, the placement of a cuffed tunneled dialysis catheter may be
appropriate, even in the acute setting. Cuffed tunneled catheters offer several advantages
when extended use is expected. They are associated with lower rates of catheter-related
bloodstream infections, owing to the presence of a subcutaneous tunnel and a Dacron cuff
that facilitates tissue ingrowth and serves as a barrier to bacterial migration.875, 911
Additionally, these catheters provide greater mechanical stability and reduce the risk of
accidental dislodgement, which is especially beneficial in children.912

such as access to interventional radiology or surgical/interventional radiology teams. In

Practice Point 5.4.8: For patients who remain dialysis dependent with low probability of
resolution of AKI or patients being prepared for hospital discharge, transition to a
cuffed tunneled catheter should be undertaken.

greater risk of mechanical complications, and poor long-term catheter function.875, 877

likelihood of resolution of AKI in the short-term, or when discharge planning begins,

5.5. Anticoagulation for RRT
Recommendation 5.5.1: Where available, we recommend using regional citrate
anticoagulation rather than heparin in children and adults receiving CRRT who do not
have contraindications to citrate (1B).
278

Evidence shows that premature filter clotting can interrupt therapy, reduce efficacy, and
increase bedside workload and costs of CRRT. As such, this recommendation places a high
value on preservation of circuit patency to ensure optimal CRRT delivery, while minimizing
the risk of circuit loss. It also places high value on balancing the benefits of anticoagulation
with the risk of adverse events due to anticoagulation, including bleeding and need for blood
transfusions. Note this recommendation applies to patients who are not receiving systemic
anticoagulation for other indications (e.g., venous thromboembolism, atrial fibrillation).

Values and preferences
The Work Group perceived that both patients and healthcare professionals would
prefer anticoagulation strategies that preserve circuit patency to ensure adequate solute
clearance, ultrafiltration, mitigation of interruptions, and minimization of adverse events
(e.g., bleeding events, metabolic complications; need for transfusion). Preservation of circuit
lifespan will also reduce bedside nursing workload, result in fewer dialysis catheter
manipulations, enable delivery of the prescribed RRT dose-intensity, and reduce the amount
of waste. At the same time, there was a strong perception for preferentially using an
anticoagulation regimen that would minimize the risk of bleeding events, particularly among
patients with increased risk (e.g., liver disease; recent surgery; coagulopathy;
thrombocytopenia, etc.). This demands that healthcare professionals balance the relative
benefits of anticoagulation (e.g., longer filter lifespan) with the potential for adverse events.
Among those at high bleeding risk, use of anticoagulant strategies with a lower risk of
bleeding or no anticoagulation may be preferable, despite the expectation of shorter circuit
lifespan. Preferences may also be shaped by institutional expertise, availability of specific
anticoagulants, clinician experience with protocols, and ability to implement appropriate
280

monitoring procedures.922 For example, RCA may be preferred in centers with adequate
training and infrastructure, whereas UFH remains commonly used due to its familiarity and
relative ease of administration.
Among pediatric intensivists and nephrologists, the preference for RCA is
particularly strong due to the frequent use of small circuits and low blood flow rates, which
predispose to clotting when no or suboptimal anticoagulation is used.923-925

T

and healthcare costs, particularly in lower resource settings. Prolonging circuit life through
effective anticoagulation reduces the frequency of circuit changes, which can in turn lower
the consumption of CRRT consumables, reduce nursing workload, and reduce waste.922 Each
F

and interrupts treatment delivery. Regional citrate anticoagulation, while associated with
longer filter lifespan and improved circuit patency, requires additional resources due to more
complex protocols, including clinicAian education and monitoring of circuit, factors that may
limit its implementation in some institutions.926 Unfractionated heparin remains widely used
due to its familiarity, ease of administration, and relatively low costs, but higher rates of
circuit failure and bleeding-related complications may increase downstream costs, including
transfusions and circuit replacement.916 This is very relevant, in particular in low-resource
R

ultimately offer favorable resource utilization by prolonging filter lifespan, reducing circuit
D

increases filter life by 17–23 hours on average compared to UFH, which translates into fewer
interruptions, reduced nursing workload, and less blood loss.915, 924, 927-930 This will reduce
need to replace circuits, for blood transfusions, and emergent catheter manipulations, all
important cost drivers for CRRT.

institutional infrastructure, local expertise, monitoring capabilities, and drug availability.931

to manage metabolic complications such as citrate accumulation or dysnatremia.915 These
requirements may be challenging in low-resource settings, which limits broader adoption
despite its clinical advantages.931 In contrast, UFH is widely available and used due to its
familiarity, ease of use, and relatively low cost,931 though it carries a higher risk of bleeding
and variable circuit lifespan.916, 931 In low-resource settings, some centers continue to operate

CRRT without anticoagulation despite shorter circuit life, due to safety concerns and cost
constraints.931

staff. Tailoring anticoagulation strategy to patient-specific bleeding risk, institutional
capability, and cost-effectiveness is essential to optimize CRRT delivery across diverse care

Practice Point 5.5.1: Unfractionated heparin, epoprostenol, or nafamostat mesylate are
acceptable alternatives to regional citrate anticoagulation for children and adults
receiving CRRT if citrate is contraindicated or not available.
In clinical scenarios where RCA cannot be used, due to contraindications (e.g., severe
liver disease, shock states) or logistical barriers, alternative anticoagulation strategies can be
employed to maintain CRRT circuit patency while minimizing the risk of complications
(e.g., bleeding).
T
Unfractionated heparin remains a commonly used alternative. However, in patients at
increased bleeding risk, its use must be weighed carefully due to the well-documented risk of
major hemorrhage with systemic anticoagulation and heparin-induced thrombocytopenia
(HIT). For such patients, nafamostat mesilate (a synthetic serine protease inhibitor) and
F
epoprostenol (a synthetic prostacyclin) have been suggested as safe and effective alternatives.
Two RCTs in adults receiving CRRT support the efficacy and safety of nafamostat mesylate
in high bleeding risk populations.932, 933 Choi et al. performed a RCT of 51 participants
receiving CRRT at a high risk of blAeeding, where nafamostat mesilate was found to reduce
clotting events without increasing bleeding complications compared to no anticoagulation.932
Lee et al. studied 73 adults receiving CRRT and found use of nafamostat mesilate resulted in
fewer clotting events (1.15 vs. 1.74 circuits/24 hours, P<0.05) and required use of fewer
filters (2.7 vs. 4.5 per patient, P=0.01) compared to those receiving no anticoagulation.933
R
These findings and a recent systematic review support nafamostat mesilate as an alternative
anticoagulant where RCA and UFH are contraindicated or unavailable.934
In children receiving CRRT, several studies demonstrate feasibility and safety of
altern D ative anticoagulation.924, 935 Miyaji et al. retrospectively compared RCA and nafamostat
mesilate in critically ill children receiving CRRT.935 Both groups achieved effective
anticoagulation with no significant differences in major bleeding, suggesting comparable
safety profiles.935 The Worldwide Exploration of Renal Replacement Outcomes
Collaborative in Kidney Disease (WE-ROCK) multinational registry included over 800
children and young adults on CRRT.936 Although anticoagulation strategies varied, centers
with experience using nafamostat mesilate or prostacyclin reported comparable survival and
filter longevity across strategies.937 Haga et al. reported that 40% of Japanese pediatric ICUs
surveyed routinely used nafamostat mesilate as the primary anticoagulant for pediatric
CRRT, reflecting national clinical acceptance in high bleeding risk patients.938
Epoprostenol, a platelet inhibitor with vasodilatory effects, is another alternative used
in both children and adults, especially when systemic anticoagulation is undesirable. While
high-quality RCT data are limited, its use has been supported by clinical experience and
observational data.918, 921, 939

Practice Point 5.5.2: The addition of regional citrate anticoagulation to systematic
anticoagulation can be appropriate for children and adults with hypercoagulable states
receiving CRRT.
Patients with systemic hypercoagulable states, such as severe COVID-19, nephrotic
syndrome, or systemic inflammatory conditions can experience recurrent circuit clotting
during CRRT, despite receiving systemic anticoagulation. In such scenarios, the addition of
RCA to ongoing systemic anticoagulation may enhance filter longevity without increasing
bleeding risk and thus be a reasonable strategy in selected children and adults.
T
A prospective multicenter study by Helms et al. involving critically ill patients with
COVID-19 and acute respiratory distress syndrome reported a high incidence of thrombotic
complications (up to 69%), even in patients already receiving systemic prophylactic or
F
therapeutic anticoagulation.940 This underscores the need to consider augmented
anticoagulation strategies for selected patients expressing hypercoagulable states receiving
extracorporeal therapies.
A
A retrospective cohort study by Valle et al. evaluated 238 ICU patients with COVID-
19-associated AKI undergoing CRRT and compared outcomes between RCA alone and RCA
plus UFH.941 The combined anticoagulation approach resulted in a ~60% reduction in the
hazard of filter clotting, without a significant increase in bleeding events.941 Similar findings
R
were reported in non-COVID hypercoagulable states. In patients with nephrotic syndrome,
Wang et al. demonstrated that the addition of LMWH to RCA during CRRT was effective in
maintaining circuit patency, with no increase in bleeding complications.942 In critically ill
children, Zhu et al. retrospectively evaluated whether either RCA alone or in combination
with D low-dose UFH was effective for preserving and prolonging filter lifespan.943 The study
found that the combined strategy improved circuit survival time without increasing
hemorrhagic risk, reinforcing the potential safety and benefit of dual anticoagulation in
hypercoagulable pediatric patients.943 These results suggest there may be a synergistic effect
between systemic and regional anticoagulation in preventing premature circuit loss in
hypercoagulable patients receiving CRRT.
Practice Point 5.5.3: Unfractionated or low-molecular-weight heparin should be
considered for children and adults receiving intermittent hemodialysis (IHD) or PIRRT
without increased bleeding when not already receiving systematic anticoagulation.
Unfractionated heparin and LMWH are widely used anticoagulants during
conventional IHD or PIRRT to prevent clotting of the extracorporeal circuit. Their use is
particularly relevant in patients who are not receiving systemic anticoagulation and do not
have an elevated bleeding risk.
287

A study by Mareedu et al. in adult patients with COVID-19 associated AKI
undergoing PIRRT found that LMWH was superior to UFH in preventing extracorporeal
circuit clotting, with a lower incidence of filter clotting events and comparable bleeding
risk.944 LMWH may be preferable due to more predictable pharmacokinetics and lower
clotting rates, but both are acceptable and widely available anticoagulation options. While
flush-only (saline) protocols may be used in patients at high bleeding risk, they are generally
associated with a higher risk of circuit clotting and treatment interruption and increase the
workload of the clinical staff.
T
Further supporting evidence comes from a large observational analysis of patients
receiving IHD and concomitant systemic antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapy.945 The
findings suggest that the use of intradialytic UFH did not significantly increase bleeding
events, even in patients on concurrent antiplatelet agents, and remained effective in
F
preserving circuit patency.945 This highlights the safety of UFH under routine conditions and
provides reassurance for its use in patients without overt bleeding risk. Although pediatric-
specific evidence is more limited, clinical practice patterns align with adults, with UFH being
routinely used in IHD and PIRRT fAor children, particularly those with stable hemodynamics
and low bleeding risk.
Practice Point 5.5.4: Anticoagulation with argatroban, danaparoid, or bivalirudin,
rather than other non-heparin anticoagulants, should be used in adults receiving RRT
R
with acute heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) who require systemic
anticoagulation.
HIT is a serious immune-mediated complication of heparin therapy that necessitates
D
immediate cessation of all heparin products and transition to alternative anticoagulation. For
patients receiving RRT, choosing an effective and safe non-heparin anticoagulant is essential
to minimize thrombotic risk and maintain circuit patency. Argatroban appears to offer the
most favorable balance of efficacy and safety, with danaparoid and bivalirudin also
considered acceptable alternatives.
Argatroban, a direct thrombin inhibitor metabolized by the liver, has been widely
studied in patients with HIT requiring RRT.946 A retrospective cohort study of 74 patients
receiving CRRT with suspected HIT found that argatroban and bivalirudin were both
effective, but argatroban had more activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT) levels within
the therapeutic range, indicating better dosing control.946 In a RCT of 66 participants
receiving CRRT with suspected HIT, argatroban was compared with lepirudin.947 Although
both anticoagulants were found to result in similar filter patency, argatroban was associated
with significantly fewer clinically relevant bleeding events (4 vs. 11; OR: 3.9; 95% CI: 1.1–
14.0; P=0.040).947
288

A Bayesian network meta-analysis evaluated various non-heparin anticoagulant
regimens for patients with HIT and suggested that argatroban was associated with the lowest
risk of bleeding and thromboembolism, as well as potentially lower mortality and shorter
hospital stays compared with alternatives, including danaparoid, lepirudin, and bivalirudin.948
Danaparoid, an indirect factor Xa inhibitor with minimal cross-reactivity to heparin-
antibodies against platelet factor 4 (PF4), also has evidence supporting its use. In a case-
series by Magnani et al. of 122 patients receiving RRT, danaparoid was found effective in
maintaining RRT circuit patency with low rates of bleeding complications.949 Bivalirudin,
T
another direct thrombin inhibitor, has also been used in RRT, although its renal clearance
necessitates dose adjustments. In comparative studies, bivalirudin has shown similar efficacy
and bleeding risk to argatroban but may be less preferred in patients with severely abnormal
kidney function due to the risk of accumulation.950
F
Practice Point 5.5.5 Regional citrate anticoagulation rather than heparin or non-
heparin anticoagulants is preferable in adults receiving RRT with subacute (HIT-A,
HIT-B) or remote HIT, who are nAot receiving systemic anticoagulation and require
anticoagulation to prevent clotting of the dialysis circuit.
In patients with a history of HIT, particularly those with subacute or remote HIT who
no longer require systemic anticoagulation, the risk of HIT recurrence remains a concern if
R
heparin is reintroduced. Additionally, non-heparin anticoagulants used systemically may
increase bleeding risk or require complex dosing adjustments. In this context, RCA offers a
safe and effective strategy for maintaining circuit patency during RRT without exposing the
patient to systemic anticoagulation risks.
D
The American Society of Hematology (ASH) 2018 Guidelines explicitly recommend
the use of RCA over heparin or other systemic non-heparin anticoagulants in this scenario.951
For patients with subacute HIT A, subacute HIT B, or remote HIT undergoing RRT and not
requiring systemic anticoagulation, the ASH Guideline panel issued a conditional
recommendation favoring RCA, despite very-low certainty of evidence, due to its favorable
safety profile and lack of systemic anticoagulant effect. The guideline emphasized that RCA
is not appropriate in acute HIT, where systemic anticoagulation is still needed.951
5.6 Hemofilters for RRT
Practice Point 5.6.1: Biocompatible membrane hemofilters and dialyzers should be used
in children and adults with AKI undergoing CRRT or IHD.
All modern hemodialyzers and filters are hollow-fiber devices constructed with
membranes made from cellulose, modified cellulose, or synthetic polymers like polysulfone
and polyethersulfone. Their core function is to clean blood by removing solute and water
through the membrane. While water removal relies on membrane hydraulic permeability, this
289

is no longer a limiting factor clinically due to automated ultrafiltration control systems,
which prevents excessive fluid removal. Consequently, manufacturers have shifted focus
toward optimizing membranes for enhanced solute exchange efficiency by increasing pore
size (e.g., high-flux membranes defined by β₂-microglobulin clearance >20 ml/min) and
engineering membrane surface area, fiber geometry, and material properties to boost
diffusive, convective, adsorptive clearance.952

T
polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA), and AN69, a copolymer of sodium methallyl sulfonate
and acrylonitrile (Table 51).953 These membranes are designed to minimize complement
activation and cytokine release, thereby reducing inflammation and improving patient
tolerance during RRT.

(CVVHD) or hemodiafiltration (CVVHDF) rather than continuous venovenous

Maintaining hemofilter and circuit patency is a key goal in CRRT, where

addition to pharmacologic anticoagulation, several non-pharmacologic strategies can

minimized.957 Optimizing predilution strategies, catheter positioning, and CRRT modality

measures are especially relevant in postoperative or bleeding-prone patients where

Practice Point 5.6.3: Where regional citrate or systemic heparin anticoagulation are

Priming the circuit with UFH can theoretically precoat the hemofilter surfaces and
tubing and mitigate early thrombus formation by inhibiting platelet and fibrin adhesion.
While the use of regional or systemic anticoagulation (e.g., RCA) often yields longer filter
survival, the use of heparin priming can provide a pragmatic and low-risk alternative,
especially for patients with contraindications to RCA or where bleeding risk limits the use of
systemic heparin.973

and those with albumin alone lasted a median of 26 minutes (IQR: 19–35 minutes), implying
the potential superior thrombo-prophylactic effect of the heparin-albumin combination.974

retrospective cohort study of 321 patients with high bleeding risk receiving 1408 IHD
T

F

utilization of heparin during IHD, with no increase in clotting or bleeding complications

5.7. Replacement and dialysate solutions for RRT
Practice Point 5.7.1: Use bicarbonate, rather than lactate, as a buffer in dialysate and
replacement fluid for CRRT in adults with AKI in the following scenarios: circulatory
R
shock, liver failure or lactic acidemia.
This practice point is reformatted from Recommendation 5.7.1 from the KDIGO 2012
Guideline.12 The guidance is based on avoiding metabolic acidosis and improving
D
hemodynamic stability of patients with conditions that may impair lactate metabolism.
Evidence from RCTs consistently shows superior metabolic and hemodynamic effects with
the use of bicarbonate-buffered compared with lactate-buffered solutions for patients
receiving CRRT.
Bicarbonate-buffered solutions are preferred due to their superior metabolic
compatibility in critically ill patients with impaired lactate metabolism. In circulatory shock,
hepatic perfusion is often reduced, impairing the capacity of the liver to convert lactate into
bicarbonate. Consequently, the use of lactate-buffered solutions may result in iatrogenic
lactate accumulation and worsen metabolic acidosis. In contrast, bicarbonate-buffered fluids
provide more predictable acid-base correction and avoid iatrogenic lactic acidosis, leading to
improved hemodynamic tolerance and more accurate interpretation of serum lactate
concentrations as a measure of end-organ perfusion.977
Four notable RCTs support the use of bicarbonate over lactate as a buffer in CRRT.
In a single center RCT by Barenbrock et al., the use of bicarbonate-buffered replacement
fluids in participants undergoing CVVH significantly improved cardiovascular stability
294

compared to lactate-buffered replacement fluids.978 Among the 117 critically ill adults
enrolled, those receiving bicarbonate-buffered solutions had lower mean blood lactate levels
(17.4 ± 8.5 mg/dl) compared to the lactate-buffered group (28.7 ± 10.4 mg/dl; P<0.05), and
higher serum bicarbonate concentrations (23.7 ± 0.4 mmol/l vs. 21.8 ± 0.5 mmol/l; P<0.01).
Importantly, the incidence of hypotensive episodes was significantly lower in the
bicarbonate-buffered group (0.26 ± 0.09 episodes per 24 h) compared to the lactate-buffered
group (0.60 ± 0.31; P<0.05). Furthermore, cardiovascular events occurred less frequently in
participants treated with bicarbonate-buffered fluids (15% vs. 38%; P<0.01).978
T
Similarly, in the study by Kierdorf et al., 12 adults with AKI receiving CVVH were
enrolled in a randomized crossover trial comparing bicarbonate- versus lactate-buffered
replacement fluids.979 The findings suggested that bicarbonate-buffered solutions provided
superior acid-base balance and hemodynamic stability. Specifically, participants receiving
F
bicarbonate-buffered solutions had significantly higher arterial pH values (mean 7.38 ± 0.04)
compared to those receiving lactate buffers (7.32 ± 0.06; P<0.05), and serum bicarbonate
levels were also higher (22 ± 3 mmol/l vs. 18 ± 4 mmol/l; P<0.05). Importantly, the
bicarbonate-buffered group experieAnced fewer hypotensive episodes (0.8 ± 0.7 vs. 2.3 ± 1.1
episodes/day; P<0.05) and lower systemic lactate levels, particularly in the context of
impaired tissue perfusion.979
In a prospective, randomized crossover trial involving 26 critically ill adults
R
undergoing CVVHD, Zimmerman et al. directly compared a bicarbonate-buffered dialysate
with a lactate-buffered dialysate over 2 48-hour treatment periods in a crossover design.980
Both solutions effectively raised serum bicarbonate from baseline (bicarbonate group:
18.9 ± 2.0 mmol/l to 22.2 ± 1.2 mmol/l; lactate group: 16.3 ± 1.5 mmol/l to 22.2 ± 1.4
D
mmol/l). Importantly, serum lactate levels differed significantly; patients dialyzed with
lactate-buffered dialysate were found to have higher serum lactate concentrations (mean
2.92 ± 0.45 mmol/l) than those treated with bicarbonate-buffered dialysate
(1.61 ± 0.25 mmol/l; P=0.01). In participants with hepatic dysfunction, lactate increased to
3.39 ± 0.68 mmol/l in participants randomized to lactate-buffered fluids compared to
1.78 ± 0.42 mmol/l in participants treated with bicarbonate-buffered fluids (P=0.036).
The risks associated with bicarbonate-buffered CRRT solutions are minimal when
used appropriately and with adequate monitoring. While lactate-based solutions may be more
readily available or less expensive in some settings, their use in high-risk populations (e.g.,
those with shock) may lead to clinically significant metabolic disturbances. Therefore, the
balance of evidence and physiological rationale favors bicarbonate-buffered solutions in
patients with circulatory shock or liver failure.608 Implementing bicarbonate-buffered
solutions in CRRT requires careful attention to infrastructure, training, and supply chain
logistics. These solutions are typically delivered in two-chamber bag systems to prevent
calcium-bicarbonate precipitation, necessitating compatible CRRT machines with
appropriate software and fluid handling capabilities.
295

In low-resource settings, where commercial CRRT solutions may not be readily
available, the implementation of bicarbonate-buffered solutions can present additional
challenges. Staff must be trained to manage the mixing process and understand the potential
implications of incorrect handling. The requirement for two-chamber bag systems or in-
house preparation to prevent calcium-bicarbonate precipitation demands appropriate
infrastructure, training, and resources that may be limited or competing with other priorities
in such environments.981 Storage requirements are more stringent than for lactate-buffered

Practice Point 5.7.2: CRRT dialysate and replacement solutions should be sterile.
Dialysate and replacement solutions for IRRT should comply with American
Association of Medical Instrumentation (AAMI) standards regarding contamination
with bacteria and endotoxins.
R
The safety and effectiveness of CRRT are dependent on the composition and quality
of dialysate and replacement solutions. In particular, the use of sterile solutions is imperative
for CRRT, as these fluids are administered directly into the extracorporeal circuit and may
D
enter the patient’s circulation either through convection or diffusion. Contaminated solutions
pose a significant risk for bloodstream infections, pyrogenic reactions, and systemic
inflammatory responses, which can adversely affect patient outcomes, especially in critically
ill populations.
IRRT should at a minimum conform to the AAMI standards for microbial
contamination and endotoxin levels. The AAMI standards recommend that dialysate used in
hemodialysis contains <200 colony-forming units (CFU)/ml of bacteria and <2 endotoxin
units (EU)/ml, with target values of <100 CFU/ml and <0.25 EU/ml for ultrapure
dialysate.982, 983 These thresholds are designed to minimize the risk of chronic inflammation
and endotoxin-related complications in patients undergoing dialysis.982, 983
In line with the ADQI consensus on precision fluid management in CRRT, the use of
biocompatible, sterile, and metabolically favorable fluids is recommended to reduce
treatment-related complications and optimize patient outcomes.984 The combination of sterile
delivery and bicarbonate buffering aligns with international best practices and enhances both
safety and efficacy in the management of AKI in critically ill populations.

5.9 Discontinuation of RRT
Practice Point 5.9.1: Discontinue RRT when it is no longer needed either because of
evidence of resolution of AKI to the point that kidney function is adequate to meet the
patient’s metabolic and fluid balance demands or because RRT is no longer consistent
with the patient’s preferences for care.
300

Most survivors of acute and critical illness complicated by AKI recover sufficient
function to become RRT independent.713, 815, 1017 Healthcare professionals should aim to
frequently and regularly evaluate for indications of AKI resolution, recovery of intrinsic
kidney function, and for the potential to feasibly discontinue RRT. This may minimize the
duration of risk associated with extracorporeal device/circuit exposure.
The decision on whether to discontinue RRT integrates several considerations,
including whether there has been sufficient resolution of AKI and recovery in intrinsic
T
kidney function to meet current demands (e.g., acid-base, electrolyte, azotemic, fluid),
whether there has been improvement in the acute illness that precipitated AKI and start of
RRT, and whether there is a commitment to ongoing RRT support if a trial of RRT
discontinuation is unsuccessful (i.e., change to goals-of-care designation).
F
Clinical trials have used clinical indications, various metabolic parameters, urine
output, and timed creatinine clearance to inform the timing of RRT discontinuation.820 The
Liberation of Acute Dialysis Trial (ALIBERATE-D) focused on hospitalized adult patients
with AKI who were hemodynamically stable and not requiring vasopressor support.1018 The
trial compared a strategy of continuing IHD for AKI only when specific metabolic or clinical
indications were met (e.g., azotemia, hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, pulmonary edema,
clinical judgement) versus continuing scheduled IHD session 3 times per week until specific
R
urine output (>1 l/d in the absence of diuretics or >2 l/d with diuretics) or creatinine
clearance (>20 ml/min) criteria were met.1018 Among the 221 participants enrolled in the
trial, the former strategy resulted in fewer dialysis sessions per week, earlier recovery to IHD
independence, and more participants achieving kidney function recovery by the time of
D
hospital discharge.
The strategy to assess for recovery of intrinsic kidney function during RRT depends
on the modality being used. For patients receiving IRRT, kidney recovery can be assessed
over interdialytic periods, whereas for patients receiving CRRT, relatively steady state can be
achieved after 24–48 hour that may enable clearer assessment (e.g., urine volume; urinary
excretion of creatinine; timed creatinine clearance, etc.).1019-1026 However, the current
literature is challenging to draw strong inferences from because “successful” RRT
discontinuation has been inconsistently defined, ranging from RRT-free between 3–90
days.1024, 1027 As such, decision-making on RRT discontinuation remains subject to wide
practice variation.1020
Practice Point 5.9.2: Clinical and metabolic parameters, interdialytic SCr changes,
urine output, fluid balance, other parameters of resolution of AKI, and residual kidney
function can be used to guide decision-making for RRT discontinuation.
301

Interdialytic periods, whether during an interruption in CRRT or between scheduled
IRRT sessions, provide an opportunity to evaluate for persistence of clinical and metabolic
indications for RRT, as well as signs of AKI resolution and kidney function recovery. In a
single center retrospective study, 448 CRRT-free intervals in 222 critically ill adult patients
receiving CRRT were evaluated to predict the suitability for a trial of CRRT
discontinuation.1025 Three parameters best predicted successful CRRT liberation: shorter total
duration of CRRT, lower Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score, and urine
output. Specifically, a higher urine output in the CRRT-free period predicted successful
liberation (66 ml/h vs. 10 ml/h).1025 Similarly, interdialytic periods between IRRT sessions
T
can be used to observe urine output and additional parameters (e.g., timed creatinine
clearance).

F
RRT discontinuation.1025, 1028-1030 In a single center in Korea, 1158 critically ill adult patients

associated with successful CRRT dAiscontinuation were greater urine output (≥300 ml/24h),

≥23 ml/min can be used to predict successful RRT discontinuation in adults.

5.12.3. RRT dose-intensity
Practice Point 5.12.3.1: An initial CRRT effluent volume for children can be 25–30
ml/kg/h. Thereafter, dose-intensity can be adjusted based on the adequacy of correction
of electrolyte, acid-base, and metabolic derangements.
D
There are no RCTs to support a target RRT dose-intensity for acutely ill or critically
ill children. Due to limited comparisons of outcomes according to delivered RRT dose-
intensity in children, this practice has generally been extrapolated from findings of RCTs
performed in adult populations.815, 820
Practice Point 5.12.3.2: The dose-intensity prescription in children receiving CRRT
should be weight-based (ml/kg/h), according to either body weight at hospital or ICU
admission or ideal body weight and not prescribed based on body surface area.
The optimal initial RRT dose-intensity in children is unknown. Survey data have
shown variation in practice, with both weight-based and body surface area (BSA)-based
methods commonly used.938, 1091 Importantly, the relationship between body weight and BSA
in children is nonlinear, presenting challenges in translating and aligning prescribed dose-
intensity between these methods, driven by large differences in age, body weight, and body
morphology across the spectrum of children encountered. For example, in neonates and
infants, an initial CRRT dose-intensity based on BSA of 2 l/h per 1.73 m2, common in survey
313

data, could translate to an unintended much higher weight-based CRRT dose-intensity of 70–
100 ml/kg/h.1091 This reinforces the necessity for routine and frequent monitoring of CRRT
(Practice Point 5.3.2 and Practice Point 5.12.3.1). Moreover, body weight at the time CRRT
is initiated may be confounded and unreliable due to acute illness and fluid accumulation,
implying body weight at hospital or ICU admission, or ideal body weight be preferentially
used.
Practice Point 5.12.3.3: In children with AKI, PD can be initiated with a fill volume of
10–20 ml/kg and short cycle times (60–90 minutes), with gradual increases in volume
T
and dwell time as tolerated, with continuous therapy over 24 hours for the first 1–3
days to achieve solute clearance and fluid balance goals.
The issue of prescribing and achieving adequate clearance using acute PD in children
F
with AKI was specifically addressed in the 2020 updated ISPD Guideline for PD in AKI
(Pediatric).776 This practice point aligns with this guideline. Age is an important determinant
of RRT modality selection in critically ill children, with many children under age 6 years
receiving acute PD.776 Acute PD caAn generally provide sufficient metabolic, azotemic and
volume control for most children, including those who are hypercatabolic.1115 Initial fill
volumes of 10–20 ml/kg are recommended to reduce the risk of dialysate leakage.1116
Thereafter, fill volume can be slowly increased to optimize solute clearance and
ultrafiltration. While initial dwell and drain times should be every 60–90 minutes, in extreme
R
and very-low birth weight infants, lower fill volumes (7–14 ml) and shorter dwell times (10–
20 minutes) may be necessary.1117

D
warranted when treating children with hyperammonemia. CRRT dose-intensity up to
200 ml/kg/h may be acceptable to achieve adequate clearance of ammonia. Children
should be appropriately monitored for unintended solute losses during periods of
augmented CRRT dose-intensity.
Ammonia can be generated in excess due to inborn errors of metabolism, poisoning,
sepsis, or acute liver failure. Ammonia is a neurotoxin and accumulation can precipitate
metabolic encephalopathy, cerebral edema, or death.1118, 1119 Serum ammonia concentrations
>100 µmol/l reliably predict severe encephalopathy among patients with acute liver
failure.1120 As such, hyperammonemia is a recognized indication for RRT.754, 1091, 1121-1128

in rebound elevation in serum ammonia.1127, 1129, 1130 Extracorporeal clearance of ammonia

capacity to achieve 50% reduction in serum ammonia concentrations within 4–9 hours.1119,

5.12.4. Catheters for RRT
Practice Point 5.12.4.1: In small children, including neonates with small vessel size, use
of a pliable cuffed catheter, as compared to a rigid temporary catheter, may be less
thrombogenic and preferred, even when the expected dialysis duration is uncertain.
The cuff can be left outside the body when the projected duration of use is short.
T
In neonates and small children requiring RRT for AKI, vascular access poses
significant challenges due to the small caliber of blood vessels, limited available access sites,
and a heightened risk of access-related complications. In this context, the use of a soft,
pliable, cuffed catheter, rather than a rigid, temporary catheter, may provide superior safety
F
and performance, even when the anticipated duration of RRT is uncertain. Rigid nontunneled
catheters, which are commonly used in adults, may be less appropriate for small children
because of an increased risk of mechanical trauma to fragile vessels, higher thrombogenicity
in low-flow states, and limited flexAibility that can lead to malposition or kinking.913, 1134
In contrast, pliable cuffed catheters made of softer materials such as silicone or
polyurethane are associated with reduced endothelial injury and a lower risk of thrombosis,
improved catheter patency in small vessels, and greater flexibility and comfort, all of which
R
help to minimize mechanical complications.1134 Although these catheters are typically
designed for long-term use, they may still offer advantages in short-term settings for neonates
and infants, especially in cases where vessel preservation is a priority. When short-term use
is anticipated, the catheter cuff can be left external to the skin to avoid subcutaneous
tunne D ling and to facilitate easier removal.1135 Additionally, cuffed catheters offer improved
securement and stability, which is especially important in infants and young children who are
more prone to accidental dislodgement.
5.12.5. Anticoagulation for RRT
Practice Point 5.12.5.1: In children requiring small catheters and circuit volume and
lower blood flow rates, anticoagulation is often necessary and preferred over no
anticoagulation during CRRT.
In critically ill children receiving CRRT, the use of anticoagulation is often necessary
due to the unique technical challenges posed by smaller catheter sizes, reduced
extracorporeal circuit volumes, and lower blood flow rates. These factors contribute to an
increased risk of circuit clotting and premature filter failure in the absence of
anticoagulation.1136, 1137 Data from a multicenter European observational cohort study
including 247 children undergoing CRRT with 1357 circuits totaling 37,562 treatment hours
(EurAKID registry) demonstrated that shorter circuit lifespan in children is influenced by
315

reduced circuit size and flow dynamics, both of which are more prone to clotting without
adequate anticoagulant support.1137
Brophy et al. conducted a multicenter evaluation highlighting how anticoagulation,
particularly RCA or UFH, used during CRRT significantly improved filter lifespan and
reduced clotting events compared to circuits managed without anticoagulation.1136 Further, in
a study of 161 children receiving CRRT (total 22,190 hours; median 74.8 hours per patient),
filter lifespan was significantly shorter for CRRT without anticoagulation, especially in
younger children and those with lower blood flow settings.1138

5.12.6. Hemofilters for RRT
Practice Point 5.12.6.1: Appropriately sized membranes proportional to body surface
area are preferred for children with AKI receiving CRRT or IRRT.
F
In children with AKI receiving CRRT or IRRT, selecting a filter membrane with a
surface area proportionate to the child’s body surface area is essential to ensure both
treatment efficacy and safety. ChildAren, particularly neonates and infants, have lower
circulating blood volumes and limited tolerance for extracorporeal circuits. Oversized
membranes can significantly increase extracorporeal volume, raising the risk of
hemodynamic instability, hypothermia, and the need for blood priming.1139
R
Coulthard and Sharp examined the feasibility and challenges of hemodialysis in very
small infants, including those weighing as little as 2.5 kg. They described the use of
extracorporeal circuits with a volume of approximately 25 ml.1139 Given that the estimated
total blood volume in infants is around 80 ml/kg, a 2.5 kg infant would have a total blood
D
volume of about 200 ml. Thus, the extracorporeal volume represents approximately 12.5% of
the infant’s total blood volume. This relatively high proportion poses significant risk for
hemodynamic instability and necessitates the use of blood priming.1139
Using an oversized membrane can lead to an increase in extracorporeal circuit
volume, which may exceed 8–10 ml/kg and increase the risk of hemodynamic instability,
particularly in neonates and small children.1140 Additionally, large membranes, even with
blood priming, can cause dilutional coagulopathy, with reductions in platelet and fibrinogen
levels due to large extracorporeal volumes. Larger membranes are also associated with
higher RRT-related macro- and micronutrient losses.1141, 1142
Conversely, undersized membranes may compromise RRT adequacy. Therefore,
proportional membrane sizing is a critical component of safe and effective CRRT delivery in
pediatric patients.1091 In the EurAKID registry, longer hemofilter lifespan was associated
with use of RCA (42 h for RCA vs. 24 h for UFH versus 18 h for no anticoagulant, P=0.001),
larger membrane surface area, and right internal jugular access (compared with left internal
jugular).1137 Notably, smaller dialyzers (membrane surface area <0.5 m2) had shorter
316

lifespans (19 h; IQR: 14–32) than both medium (membrane surface area 0.5–1.0 m2–26 h;
IQR: 16–43, P=0.04) and large dialyzers (membrane surface area >1.0 m2–33 h; IQR: 20–48,

Pediatric-specific CRRT machines and miniaturized circuits are available and have

T
membrane surface area may be increased as clinically indicated. Importantly, the advantages

F
be clinically significant. In addition, these systems support lower blood flow rates, which
