# Chapter 3: Prevention and Treatment of AKI

Numerous prevention and treatment strategies have been investigated for AKI. Given
the central role of the kidneys in regulating fluid balance and BP, derangements in these
parameters are closely associated with the development of AKI. Much of the evidence
generated to prevent and mitigate AKI in acutely ill patients has focused on hemodynamic
T
management strategies, including intravenous fluids and vasoactive/inotropic support, guided
by clinical assessment (mental state, heart rate, respiratory rate, peripheral perfusion,
capillary return, skin turgor, oedema), physiological and biochemical information (urine
output, SCr, lactate concentration, biomarkers, and pH), and hemodynamic parameters
F
(cardiac output, BP, central venous pressure, pulse pressure variation, stroke volume
variation).
AKI is not a uniform entity. While many aspects of care are applicable to prevention
A
and management of all causes of AKI, some strategies are specific to particular etiologies.
Distinct forms of AKI include AKI that occurs in the perioperative setting, hepatorenal
syndrome, tumor lysis syndrome, rhabdomyolysis, and pregnancy-associated AKI.
R
Current evidence for AKI prevention and management has primarily been shaped by
research on hospital-acquired AKI in high-resource settings. This knowledge often does not
fully address the unique risk factors, diagnostic challenges, socioeconomic aspects, and
resource limitations in low- and middle-resource settings. Addressing community-acquired
AKI Drequires public health-oriented strategies including community education, improved
access to clean water, early recognition and referral systems, and scalable, low-cost treatment
options alongside individualized clinical management.
3.1 Fluid management
Recommendation 3.1.1: We recommend using crystalloids rather than colloids
(albumin, gelatin, or starches) as initial management for intravascular volume
expansion in children and adults at risk of or with AKI or AKD (1B).
This recommendation puts high value on evidence from large RCTs consistently
demonstrating superiority of crystalloids over artificial colloids and equivalence with natural
colloids (e.g., albumin) for preventing AKI, need for RRT, and mortality. It also places value
on the lower cost of crystalloids compared with colloids. It places a lower value on the
potentially greater, but short-lived hemodynamic effects of colloids. It does not apply to
people with hemorrhagic shock, in whom blood products are appropriate for resuscitation.

Values and preferences
This recommendation places a high valueF on minimizing the risk of AKI or AKD,
and on the use of interventions with the most favorable balance of benefits to risks. The
Work Group judged that, for most people, the potential kidney and systemic harms
associated with the use of colloids, especially synthetic starches, outweigh any uncertain or
A
modest benefits related to intravascular volume expansion.
The recommendation also reflects a preference for crystalloids because they are
widely available, familiar to healthcare professionals, less costly, and supported by evidence
suggesting comparabRle or superior safety with respect to kidney outcomes. The Work Group
recognized that some patients or healthcare professionals may value potential short-term
hemodynamic effects of colloids or have specific clinical circumstances where albumin is
considered. However, given the low certainty of evidence for benefit and the consistent
signal for harm with certain colloids, most well-informed patients would likely prefer
D
crystalloids as initial therapy to reduce kidney-related risks. Specific people (e.g., Jehovah’s
witnesses) may object to receiving albumin because it is derived from human blood.
Resource use and costs
The Work Group considered resource utilization and costs to be important factors in
formulating this recommendation. Crystalloids are inexpensive, widely available across a
broad range of healthcare settings, and do not require specialized storage or administration,
making them feasible for use in both high- and low-resource settings. Albumin is
significantly more expensive than crystalloids and requires human plasma for production.
Artificial colloids, including gelatins and starches, are more expensive than crystalloids and
are not available everywhere.
Use of some colloids is also associated with increased downstream resource
utilization due to higher rates of RRT, which may further increase overall healthcare costs.
Avoiding these colloids as initial therapy may therefore reduce both direct acquisition costs
and indirect costs related to complications and prolonged hospitalization. Overall, routine use
121

of more expensive colloid solutions does not represent a cost-effective use of healthcare
resources.
Considerations for implementation
Implementation of this recommendation requires ensuring that crystalloids are readily
available and prioritized as the default fluids for initial intravascular volume expansion.
Institutional protocols, order sets, and clinical pathways should reflect crystalloids as first-
line therapy, with clear guidance on fluid selection and correcTt use. Education of clinicians,
nurses, and pharmacists is important to reinforce the rationale for avoiding colloids in this
population and to promote consistent practice across care settings. The Work Group
acknowledges that albumin may still be considered in specific clinical circumstances or
disease states based on healthcare professional juFdgment or other guideline
recommendations;252, 321, 322 such use should be clearly justified and documented. Protocols
and pathways should indicate that synthetic starches should be avoided.

Recommendation 3.1.2: We recommend using buffered crystalloids instead of 0.9%
saline for volume expansion in the absence of traumatic brain injury in children and
adults at risk of or with AKI or AKD (1B).

treatment with RRT with buffered crystalloids compared to 0.9% saline. It places a lower
value on their accessibility and potentially higher costs. This recommendation does not apply
to people with traumatic brain injury, in whom 0.9% saline may be preferred due to concerns
about the impact on intracranial pressure with solutions of lower serum osmolality, or choice

Values and preferences
From the point of view of patients, the use of one crystalloid type versus another feels
identical. Most well-informed patients would preFfer an option where the totality of evidence
suggests it may have a small effect on lowering mortality, reduces the incidence of AKI, and
avoids metabolic complications such as hyperchloremia and metabolic acidosis, which may
precipitate the need for RRT.
A
The Work Group recognized that healthcare professionals and patients may value the
simplicity, availability, and perceived safety of 0.9% saline, and that individual clinical
circumstances; such as traumatic brain injury, specific electrolyte abnormalities, or local
resource constraints;R may influence fluid choice. Therefore, it is logical to support the
administration of buffered solutions, except for situations where their administration could be
harmful (e.g., in people at risk of or with traumatic brain injury or cerebral edema) or they
remain relatively unstudied (e.g., tumor lysis syndrome prophylaxis), or when the
administration of such fluids might delay care (e.g. during shock or crush injury, when a
D
delay in care may be detrimental).
Resource use and costs
Some buffered crystalloids such as lactate Ringer’s solution or Hartmann’s solution
are widely available and have costs that are similar to 0.9% saline in many jurisdictions,
though costs up to 1.1–2 times higher than 0.9% saline have been described in low- and
middle-resource settings.356 However, Plasma-Lyte is less widely accessible, and its price
varies between countries and can be substantially higher than 0.9% saline. While the
magnitude of benefit of buffered solutions in terms of RRT, MAKE, or mortality is low, over
30 million patients receive intravenous fluids every year, making fluid therapy a fundamental
component of acute care.357 From a population health perspective, the benefit of even small
effects on clinical outcomes such as RRT and mortality is significant and supportive of cost
utility. Further, use of buffered crystalloids may reduce the incidence of hyperchloremic
metabolic acidosis and related complications, which can prompt additional laboratory testing,
therapeutic interventions, and prolonged hospitalization, further supporting cost-
effectiveness.

Rationale
Fluid therapy is a key component of the prevention and management of AKI and
AKD, and the composition of administered fluids may influence kidney outcomes and
mortality. Saline (0.9% saline) contains a supraphysiologic chloride concentration, which can
lead to hyperchloremia, metabolic acidosis, and renal vasoconstriction, potentially reducing
kidney perfusion and contributing to AKI.
Buffered crystalloids more closely approximate the electrolyte composition of plasma
and include metabolizable anions that reduce the chloride load and help maintain acid–base
balance. RCTs and meta-analyses have demonstrated lower rates of AKI outcomes and
126

mortality in adults and lower incidence of RRT and AKI in children with use of buffered
crystalloids compared with 0.9% saline. Overall, outside of specific situations (fluid
administration in traumatic brain injury, and volume expansion for prevention of tumor lysis,
heme pigment- and contrast-associated AKI), buffered crystalloids offer better kidney and
patient outcomes than 0.9% saline for children and adults, though availability may be limited
and cost implications are relatively greater for these solutions in low- and middle-resource
settings.

Recommendation 3.1.3: For children and adults with AKI or AKD who have severe
metabolic acidosis with acidemia (pH <7.20), we suggest the use of intravenous
bicarbonate over RRRT unless there is another urgent indication for RRT (2C).
Intravenous sodium bicarbonate can correct metabolic acidosis, attenuate hyperkalemia, and
potentially stabilize hemodynamics, thereby decreasing the application of RRT. However,
RRT should be initiated promptly if there is another urgent indication, such as refractory
D
hyperkalemia, severe volume overload, or symptomatic uremia. Healthcare professionals
should monitor for potential complications of bicarbonate therapy, including volume
expansion, hypernatremia, and paradoxical intracellular acidosis.

Balance of benefits and harms

corrected medically. Sodium bicarbonate therapy is typically administered as either an
isotonic infusion or a hypertonic 4.2% or 8.4% infusion. However, these solutions may be
associated with complications related to volume overload and shifts in systemic pH. Potential
benefits with the use of sodium bicarbonate therapy in this setting are improvement in pH
and hyperkalemia as well as a better response to vasoactive medications.

Practice Point 3.2.1: Use hemodynamic monitoring strategies for adults at risk of and
with AKI to assess adequate organ perfusion and oxygen delivery, which will be
context-sensitive and resource availability dependent.
Hemodynamic monitoring is intended to ensure maintenance of adequate
intravascular volume, cardiac output, and tissue oxygenation.380 Autoregulatory mechanisms
regulate kidney blood flow within a broad range of pressures Tto preserve a stable GFR.
Preexisting conditions including CKD and hypertension, and events including sepsis, major
surgery and AKI itself, may disrupt kidney autoregulation, contributing to ischemia and
AKI.381 Recognition and correction of inadequate organ perfusion are central to AKI
prevention and mitigation in vulnerable people. HFemodynamic monitoring provides a
framework to guide timely and individualized interventions aimed at maintaining sufficient
kidney blood flow and systemic oxygen delivery while avoiding harm from over-
resuscitation. Large observational studies have shown that the degree and duration of
A
hypotension during major surgery and critical illness are associated with AKI and major
adverse kidney events.382, 383 The Intraoperative Norepinephrine to Control Arterial Pressure
(INPRESS) study reported a significantly lower risk of postoperative organ dysfunction,
including AKI in adults who received an individualized BP management strategy compared
to standard BP manaRgement during major surgery.380 However, subsequent RCTs did not
confirm these findings.384-386
The choice and intensity of hemodynamic monitoring should be tailored to the
clinical context, severity of illness, and available resources. In all settings, careful clinical
D
assessment, including trends in BP, heart rate, urine output, mental status, capillary refill, and
peripheral perfusion are fundamental. Where available, adjunctive tools for continuous BP
monitoring, pulse oximetry, serum lactate measurement, and bedside ultrasound can enhance
assessment of intravascular volume status, cardiac function, and organ perfusion. In higher-
acuity environments, advanced (invasive or minimally invasive) hemodynamic monitoring
(e.g., arterial catheters, central venous pressure monitoring, or advanced cardiac output
monitoring) may be considered to support complex decision-making.
Establishing advanced hemodynamic monitoring is relatively common in high
resource settings.387 Invasive devices like pulmonary artery catheters are associated with
potential complications, including thrombosis, bleeding, and infection, which may negatively
affect patients’ outcomes and increase costs and resource utilization. Increasingly, less
invasive tools like echocardiography are used to determine cardiac output and monitor
haemodynamics.387

resource use and longer stay in the ICU and hospital. Therefore, the choice of hemodynamic
monitoring strategy should balance goals to optimize perfusion while minimizing iatrogenic
injury. In resource-limited settings, reliance on structured clinical assessment and basic
monitoring can still support effective hemodynamic management and AKI risk reduction.

3.7 Tumor lysis syndrome-associated AKI
Practice Point 3.7.1: Volume expansion without urinary alkalinization using
intravenous crystalloids should be done in children and adults with or at risk of tumor
lysis syndrome.
R
Tumor lysis syndrome can occur spontaneously or in the setting of chemotherapy,
typically in the context of leukemias/lymphomas and more rarely in solid cancers with
relatively high cell turnover rates or massive cell burden. Uric acid from lysed tumor cells
contributes to the pathogenesis of AKI due to precipitation in kidney tubules. Precipitation of
D
calcium phosphate in renal tubules as a result of hyperphosphatemia may also be
contributory. Hyperuricemia may also lead to decreased kidney blood flow through
vasoconstriction and impaired autoregulation.468 Hyperkalemia and metabolic acidosis from
lysed cells can be life-threatening and necessitate urgent RRT. A number of classifications
have been proposed to risk-stratify cancers into low, intermediate, and high risk for tumor
lysis syndrome.469, 470
Consequently, efforts to prevent AKI are important for children with diseases or
treatments carrying intermediate or high risks of tumor lysis syndrome. A predominant focus
is on mitigating uric acid precipitation in kidney tubules. This can be aided by volume
expansion, with the goal of increasing urine flow to at least 100 ml/hour in adults and
preventing tubular obstruction, along with measures to decrease systemic uric acid
concentrations. Crystalloids are recommended for volume expansion, although there is lack
of evidence to support the selection of one fluid over another in this context. There is no

Practice Point 3.7.2: In children and adults at lower risk of tumor lysis syndrome, or

Rasburicase is a recombinant form of urate oxidase, which metabolizes uric acid to the much
more soluble allantoin. In contrast, allopurinol and febuxostat inhibit xanthine oxidase to

allopurinol and febuxostat are not effective when uric acid is already present since these
agents do not lower uric acid concentration. Rasburicase may prevent the need for RRT in

Key information
D
Balance of benefits and harms
The majority of evidence for prophylaxis and treatment of tumor lysis syndrome
comes from studies in children, since they are more likely to have cancers that put them at
high risk of tumor lysis syndrome. Rasburicase is a recombinant urate oxidase that
enzymatically converts uric acid to allantoin, a highly soluble metabolite readily excreted by
the kidney. This mechanism allows rapid and profound reduction in serum uric acid levels,
including clearance of preexisting hyperuricemia. The side effect profile of rasburicase
includes disseminated intravascular coagulopathy and severe allergies. People with G6PD
deficiency are at risk of methemoglobinemia. In contrast, allopurinol and febuxostat inhibit
xanthine oxidase and reduce the formation of new uric acid but do not eliminate circulating
uric acid already present at the time of treatment initiation. Their onset of action is slower,
and they may lead to accumulation of xanthine and hypoxanthine.
Meta-analysis of 4 RCTs comparing rasburicase versus allopurinol in children with
tumor lysis syndrome showed that those treated with rasburicase had a significantly lower
risk of needing RRT (RR: 0.27; 95% CI: 0.10–0.76) and a significantly lower risk of tumor
170

lysis syndrome related mortality (RR: 0.05; 95% CI: 0.0–0.90) but also a significantly higher
risk of serious adverse events (RR: 6.20; 95% CI: 1.14–33.65), which included disseminated
intravascular coagulopathy and methemoglobinemia (Supplementary Table S61).471-475
In people with G6PD deficiency, the generation of hydrogen peroxide as part of the
metabolism of uric acid by rasburicase can cause severe hemolytic anemia and
methemoglobinemia. Thus, screening for G6PD deficiency is recommended in newborns and
individuals of African, Asian, Mediterranean, or Middle EasteTrn descent before
administration when feasible. Allergic reactions are also relatively common.
The evidence for prevention and management of tumor lysis syndrome in adults is
limited. The challenge with extrapolating the effeFcts of these agents on adults is that adult
populations are generally at lower risk of life-threatening tumor lysis syndrome. Rasburicase
is often reserved for administration in adults only when uric acid levels exceed a
predetermined threshold because of higher cost and risks of adverse reactions.
A
In people at low risk of tumor lysis syndrome, or when rasburicase is contraindicated
or unavailable, xanthine oxidase inhibitors may be used. Allopurinol can be considered in
those with low or intermediate risk of tumor lysis syndrome. Prescribers should be aware of
the high incidence oRf skin reactions with allopurinol. Screening for allopurinol
hypersensitivity with the HLA-B*58:01 genetic allele should be considered before initiating
therapy, when possible, particularly for people of Han Chinese, Thai, or Korean descent.
Hypersensitivity reactions, including Stevens-Johnson syndrome, are also more common
with allopurinol. Finally, allopurinol has a number of important drug-drug interactions that
D
must be considered during its administration.
An alternative xanthine oxidase inhibitor with fewer interactions is febuxostat.
However, there have been reports of hypersensitivity reactions, and an increased risk of
mortality from major cardiovascular events with febuxostat. The safety and efficacy of
febuxostat in children has not been established.
Certainty of evidence
There is moderate certainty of evidence that, in children with tumor lysis syndrome,
rasburicase greatly reduces the risk of death due to tumor lysis syndrome and the risk of AKI
requiring RRT compared with allopurinol, but with no evidence of an effect on overall
mortality.471-475 However, rasburicase increases the risk of serious adverse events compared
with allopurinol. The certainty of evidence was downgraded for very serious methodological
limitations across trials but was upgraded due to the very large effect sizes for most
outcomes. The certainty of evidence for use of rasburicase in adults with tumor lysis
171

syndrome was downgraded to low due to indirectness, since there were no eligible trials in
adults.
Values and preferences
This recommendation places a high value on the prevention of life-threatening
complications of tumor lysis syndrome, including AKI requiring RRT, but also cardiac
arrhythmias, seizures, and death, through rapid and effective reduction of serum uric acid
levels. The Work Group judged that patients and healthcare pTrofessionals would generally
prioritize immediate and reliable uric acid control in those at high risk or with tumor lysis
syndrome over considerations of higher acquisition cost and the need for parenteral
administration associated with rasburicase.
F
However, this recommendation also places a high value on safety in people with
G6PD deficiency, in whom rasburicase may cause severe hemolysis and
methemoglobinemia. In settings where G6PD status is unknown and testing is not readily
A
available; healthcare professionals and patients may choose alternative urate-lowering
strategies until deficiency is excluded.
Resource use and costs
Cost, availabRility, and healthcare system protocols may influence agent selection and
dosing strategies. The Work Group recognized that the cost of rasburicase is greater than that
of allopurinol or feboxustat on a per-dose or per-treatment-course basis. Further, rasburicase
requires intravenous administration and appropriate laboratory handling of blood samples on
ice for uric acid measurement, which may increase direct and indirect healthcare costs
D
compared with orally administered alternatives.
The prevention of AKI requiring RRT with its associated costs may make use of
rasburicase cost-effective. The Work Group recognized that rasburicase is substantially more
expensive than xanthine oxidase inhibitors (allopurinol and febuxostat) on a per-dose and
per-treatment-course basis. However, a cost effectiveness analysis from the U.S. suggested
that rasburicase was associated with significantly shorter ICU and overall hospital stays and
lower total inpatient costs than allopurinol for people with tumor lysis syndrome.476 Cost-
effectiveness is likely to vary according to baseline tumor lysis syndrome risk, local drug
pricing, dosing strategies, and healthcare system structure. In people at low risk of tumor
lysis syndrome, the incremental benefit of rasburicase over allopurinol or febuxostat is likely
small, and the higher cost may not be justified. In contrast, in people at high risk of tumor
lysis syndrome or with marked hyperuricemia, rasburicase is more likely to be cost-effective
due to reduction in downstream complications.
172

Availability of rasburicase may be limited in some regions, and access to G6PD
testing may further influence resource use. In resource-constrained settings, cost and access
considerations may necessitate prioritization of rasburicase for those at highest risk, with
allopurinol or febuxostat used in lower-risk patients.
Considerations for implementation
Successful implementation of this recommendation requires systematic risk
stratification for tumor lysis syndrome in children and adults Twith malignancy, ideally prior
to initiation of cytotoxic therapies. Institutions should establish standardized protocols to
identify people at intermediate and high risk of tumor lysis syndrome. Early recognition is
essential to allow timely administration of rasburicase before or at the onset of biochemical
abnormalities. F
Rasburicase should not be administered to people with G6PD deficiency due to the
risk of severe hemolysis and methemoglobinemia. Screening for G6PD deficiency should be
A
performed in populations with higher prevalence, when feasible, and urgently when clinical
suspicion exists. In situations where G6PD status is unknown and testing is not immediately
available, healthcare professionals should carefully weigh risks and benefits, particularly in
people at lower risk.
R
Healthcare systems should ensure availability of rasburicase in centers treating people
at high risk of tumor lysis syndrome. Institutional protocols may include predefined dosing to
optimize efficacy and cost-effectiveness. Clear guidance regarding repeat dosing criteria
based on serial uric acid measurements and clinical response should be established.
D
Appropriate laboratory handling of blood samples is critical when monitoring uric
acid levels in people receiving rasburicase, as ex vivo uric acid degradation can lead to falsely
low measurements. Blood samples should be collected in prechilled tubes, transported on ice,
and analyzed promptly according to manufacturer and laboratory guidance.
Rasburicase should be integrated into a comprehensive tumor lysis syndrome
prevention and management strategy that includes intravenous hydration, careful monitoring
of electrolytes and kidney function, and prompt management of associated electrolytes
abnormalities including hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, and hypocalcemia.
Interdisciplinary coordination among oncology, nephrology, pharmacy, and laboratory
services is essential.
Rationale
The recommendation to use rasburicase in children and adults at risk or with tumor
lysis syndrome is based on the effectiveness at preventing the need for RRT but close
173

monitoring for serious adverse effects is needed. Uric acid plays a central role in the
pathogenesis of tumor lysis syndrome-associated AKI through intratubular crystal
precipitation, tubular obstruction, and renal vasoconstriction. The risk and severity of AKI
correlate with the magnitude and rapidity of uric acid elevation. Accordingly, prompt, and
effective urate control is a cornerstone of tumor lysis syndrome prevention and management.
RCTs and comparative studies in both pediatric and adult populations with
hematologic malignancies consistently demonstrate that rasbuTricase lowers uric acid more
rapidly and more completely than allopurinol. Evidence also supports reduced rates of
laboratory tumor lysis syndrome and need for RRT with rasburicase in high-risk populations.
In people at low or intermediate risk of tuFmor lysis syndrome, the absolute risk of
severe hyperuricemia and AKI is lower. In this setting, prophylactic reduction of uric acid
production with allopurinol or febuxostat is generally sufficient, and the incremental benefit
of rasburicase is likely small.
A
The recommendation therefore reflects a risk-adapted approach. In children and
adults at high risk of tumor lysis syndrome, or with established laboratory or clinical tumor
lysis syndrome, the benefits of rapid and reliable urate reduction with rasburicase outweigh
considerations relateRd to cost and intravenous administration. In contrast, for those at low or
intermediate risk, xanthine oxidase inhibitors represent reasonable and effective alternatives
for prevention.

D
Practice Point 3.8.1: Use intravenous crystalloids for volume expansion in children and
adults at risk of or with heme pigment nephropathy due to rhabdomyolysis or
hemolysis.
Myoglobin and hemoglobin are endogenous nephrotoxic substances that can
precipitate in kidney tubules and cause acute tubular injury in the setting of rhabdomyolysis
and intravascular hemolysis, respectively. People with rhabdomyolysis and hemolysis should
be monitored closely for the development of hyperkalemia or metabolic acidosis that also
accompany muscle and breakdown of red blood cells and may increase the risk of requiring
RRT. There are no specific pharmacotherapies to enhance metabolism of myoglobin or
hemoglobin. The mainstay of therapy is volume expansion to prevent precipitation in kidney
tubules, with close monitoring for fluid overload, which may develop rapidly if patients
become oliguric. There is limited data to support the selection of one crystalloid fluid over
another in this setting.
174

In people with rhabdomyolysis, the use of isotonic bicarbonate solutions has been
proposed to increase the solubility of myoglobin but the limited data do not support this
practice.477, 478 A strategy to augment urine output using mannitol or loop diuretics is not
advised.
3.9 Nutritional intervention in AKI
Practice Point 3.9.1: Nutrition should be provided preferentially via the enteral route in
children and adults at risk of or with AKI or AKD. T
At present, there is limited data to guide the provision of nutritional support in adults
with or at risk of AKI or AKD.479 Many nutrition guidelines are largely opinion based.
Despite these limitations, it is clear that enteral nFutrition is the preferred form of nutritional
support for hospitalized children and adults, with provision via a feeding tube if oral feeding
is not possible. Enteral nutrition is thought to decrease gut atrophy and maintain gut integrity,
which decreases bacterial and endotoxin translocation.
A
AKI is a major risk factor for gastrointestinal bleeding, and enteral nutrition is
thought to reduce the risk of stress ulcers and consequently gastrointestinal bleeding.
Although the enteral route is preferred, more recent clinical trials (Enteral versus parenteral
early nutrition in venRtilated adults with shock [NUTRIREA-2]) suggest that the risks of
parenteral nutrition (e.g., infection) are often overestimated, so if the enteral route cannot be
used or enteral nutrition is not tolerated, parenteral nutrition is preferable to no nutrition.480
Practice Point 3.9.2: Avoid restriction of protein intake with the aim of preventing or
D
delaying initiation of RRT in children and adults with AKI or AKD.
Protein hypercatabolism, driven by inflammation, stress and acidosis, is common
among critically ill children and adults. Those with AKI are at high risk of malnutrition but
the optimal amount of protein supplementation is unknown. Nutritional management should
aim to supply sufficient protein to maintain metabolic balance. There is little evidence that
hypercatabolism can be overcome simply by increasing protein intake.
Restricting protein can exacerbate muscle wasting and malnutrition, leading to poor
outcomes. Studies show that protein intake does not delay kidney recovery but rather
supports growth and healing.481 Hence, nutritional protein administration should not be
restricted as a means to attenuate the rise in blood urea nitrogen associated with declining
GFR.
Children with AKI are at high risk of protein-energy malnutrition, particularly in ICU
settings, where nutritional deficits can weaken immune function and prolong
175

hospitalization.482 Guidelines from the Pediatric Renal Nutrition Taskforce and the ADQI
recommend age-appropriate protein and caloric intake rather than generalized restrictions,
ensuring that children receive sufficient nutrition to prevent deficits and support recovery.483
Practice Point 3.9.3: Excessive protein intake should be avoided in adults with AKI or
AKD.
Given the association of protein-calorie malnutrition wTith adverse outcomes in
critically ill adults, there has been significant interest in protein supplementation. A large,
pragmatic RCT compared the impact of high protein intake (≥2.2 g/kg/day) versus usual
protein intake (≤1.2 g/kg/day) in critically ill adults on mechanical ventilation.484 There was
no difference in the proportion of participants disFcharged alive by day 60 (46.1% in the high
protein arm vs. 50.2% in the usual protein arm, p=0.27). However, participants with AKI
who were randomized to high protein intake had a higher mortality and longer time to
hospital discharge.
A
In a post hoc analysis of the Nephro-Protective trial, participants with normal baseline
kidney function had lower mortality with amino acid supplementation to achieve a total
protein intake of 2 g/kg/day, whereas those with abnormal kidney function at study entry did
not have harm from Ramino acid supplementation, but also had no benefit.485
These trials suggest that excessive protein intake should be avoided in adults with
AKI or AKD. Although protein and amino acid losses increase with continuous renal
replacement therapy (CRRT) and peritoneal dialysis (PD), the extent depends on the dialysis
D
prescription. It is also unclear how protein intake should be adjusted.
Practice Point 3.9.4: Caloric targets do not need to be changed due to AKI or AKD in
children or adults, unless they are receiving RRT.
Adequate nutritional support is important to support recovery from critical illness.
Although many diseases associated with AKI or AKD (e.g., sepsis) will increase metabolic
demand and catabolism, metabolic requirements should take into consideration the disease
processes associated with AKI or AKD. Caloric targets do not need to be restricted in the
context of AKI or AKD. When feasible, indirect calorimetry should be considered to
measure energy expenditure and metabolic rates. Furthermore, to avoid overfeeding, calories
provided from RRT fluids and citrate should be incorporated into caloric targets for children
and adults receiving RRT.
176

Practice Point 3.9.5: Regular review and supplementation of water-soluble vitamins and
micronutrients should be considered in children and adults receiving continuous RRT
(CRRT).
CRRT is associated with increased loss of water soluble vitamins (B complex
vitamins and vitamin C) and micronutrients in the effluent fluid.486 In a scoping review that
analyzed 35 publications with focus on micronutrient loss during RRT, carnitine, copper, and
selenium along with vitamins B1 and C were identified as miTcronutrients where
deficiency/loss was commonly observed.487 Because of variability in CRRT modality, dose,
membrane characteristics, duration of therapy, nutritional intake, and patient-specific factors,
the risk and magnitude of micronutrient losses are heterogeneous. Routine supplementation
with standard multivitamin preparations may notF fully compensate for ongoing losses in
people receiving prolonged or high-effluent-dose CRRT, while indiscriminate high-dose
supplementation without monitoring may risk accumulation of certain trace elements.
A
Monitoring strategies should be individualized based on duration and intensity of
CRRT, nutritional status, and clinical course. Measurement of selected micronutrients (e.g.,
thiamine, vitamin C, selenium, zinc) may be considered in people receiving prolonged
CRRT, those with limited nutritional intake, or when clinical features raise suspicion of
deficiency. R
In settings where laboratory measurement is not readily available, empiric
supplementation with water-soluble vitamins at doses exceeding standard daily requirements
may be reasonable, particularly for thiamine. However, whether routine supplementation is
D
beneficial has not been investigated in an RCT.
3.10 AKI in pregnancy
Practice Point 3.10.1: Pregnant women with AKI or AKD require specific treatments,
including consideration of early delivery.
AKI during pregnancy and following childbirth is an important obstetric
complication, with distinct etiologies shaped by the interplay of maternal physiology and
pregnancy-specific pathologies. Globally, pregnancy-related AKI affects approximately 2%
of pregnancies. with incidences as low as 0.08%–0.12% in high income countries.488
However, rates remain 20–100-fold higher in low- and middle-resource settings.489 In the
U.S., pregnancy-related AKI incidence tripled between 2006 and 2015, fueled by rising
maternal age and comorbidities, including diabetes and hypertension.490 Racial inequities
persist, with Black and Native American women facing 52% and 45% higher risks,
respectively, compared to White women.
177

The causes of AKI in pregnancy may be due to pregnancy specific disorders,
including preeclampsia, hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, low platelets (HELLP)
syndrome, acute fatty liver of pregnancy, thrombotic microangiopathies, severe hemorrhage,
sepsis, and factors independent of pregnancy. These conditions may progress rapidly and are
associated with substantial maternal and fetal morbidity and mortality. Identifying the
underlying etiology is important to guide management, including consideration of early
delivery.
T
Management of AKI or AKD in pregnancy differs from nonpregnant populations in
several important respects.491 Maternal stabilization must occur concurrently with continuous
assessment of fetal well-being. Volume management requires careful attention to the altered
physiologic state of pregnancy and the risk of puFlmonary edema, particularly in
preeclampsia. Blood pressure targets may differ, and medications must be selected with
consideration of fetal safety. Nephrotoxic agents and drugs contraindicated in pregnancy
should be avoided.
A
In severe preeclampsia, HELLP syndrome, or acute fatty liver of pregnancy, maternal
abnormal kidney function may improve only after delivery and delivery of the fetus and
placenta represents definitive therapy. Similarly, persistent maternal deterioration (e.g.,
worsening kidney fuRnction, refractory hypertension, pulmonary edema, or progressive
thrombocytopenia) or evidence of fetal compromise may necessitate early delivery,
balancing gestational age, and neonatal risks against maternal safety. The timing of delivery
should be individualized and determined collaboratively by obstetrics, maternal–fetal
medicine, nephrology, neonatology, and critical care teams.492, 493
D
RRT is required in 30% of women with pregnancy-related AKI for maternal or fetal
indications. When indicated, RRT should not be delayed due to pregnancy. Intensified
dialysis regimens may be required to optimize maternal metabolic control and improve fetal
outcomes. Anticoagulation strategies and modality selection should consider bleeding risk,
hemodynamic stability, and gestational age.
3.11 Environmental and tropical AKI
Practice Point 3.11.1: For children and adults presenting with AKD or AKI in tropical
and subtropical regions, evaluate for region-specific infectious and environmental
causes and initiate early disease-specific therapy in addition to standard supportive
management of AKI.
Given the diverse etiologies of tropical AKI, including infectious and environmental
causes (e.g., malaria, leptospirosis, dengue, scrub typhus, envenomation, hemolysis,
rhabdomyolysis, and diarrheal illness), early recognition through clinical suspicion and

targeted diagnostic tests, prompt kidney protective measures and use of appropriate infection-

sanitation, vector control), regulation of traditional remedies, and expanding access to
effective medical care, especially in resource-limited settings.

A

Practice Point 3.11.3: For children and adults presenting with AKD or AKI
following prolonged exposure to high temperatures, consider heat injury as a cause
of AKI.
Heat injury, especially from heatstroke, can lead to AKI through dehydration,
hypotension, and direct tubular damage.501-503 Outdoor workers, particularly in tropical
regions, are highly vulnerable due to prolonged exposure to high temperatures and intense
physical labor. Those with limited access to water face an even greater risk. Workers in
construction, agriculture, and similar fields often lack consistent breaks or hydration
T
resources, especially in areas with poor infrastructure or employer oversight. Climate
change makes this worse by driving up temperatures and the frequency of heatwaves,
putting these workers at higher risk of heat-related kidney damage. Recurrent episodes of
AKI can progress to CKD and have been implicated in the genesis of CKD of unknown
F
etiology, especially in Central America and South Asia.504, 505
Prevention entails practical steps like staying hydrated by drinking water
regularly, avoiding strenuous work during the hottest parts of the day, taking rest breaks
A
in shaded areas, and gradually acclimating to hot environments to build tolerance. For
outdoor workers, employers should provide water access, enforce breaks, and train staff
to spot early heat stress signs like dizziness.

3.12.1 Hemodynamic management
Practice Point 3.12.1.1: Use age-appropriate blood pressure targets to guide
hemodynamic management in children at risk or with AKI.
D
Avoidance of hypotension is a critical component of hemodynamic management.
The Surviving Sepsis Campaign defines pediatric hypotension by age-based systolic
thresholds (e.g., <50 mm Hg for infants <12 months, <60 mm Hg for children 1–5 years,
and <70 mm Hg for those >5 years), which are used to trigger resuscitative efforts in
septic shock and have been widely adopted in pediatric critical care.323
Even brief durations of profound hypotension, defined by systolic BP below the
first percentile for age, sex, and height, are independently associated with increased risk
of new or persistent AKI in children with sepsis. In a large retrospective cohort study, the
odds of developing Stage 2 or 3 AKI increased by 9% for every doubling in time spent
under this severe hypotensive threshold, independent of other risk factors.506 This
association was not observed when hypotension was defined by Pediatric Advanced Life
Support thresholds, suggesting that individualized, percentile-based targets may be more
sensitive for identifying kidney hypoperfusion risk. Additionally, longer duration of
hypotension was associated with fewer AKI-free days and delayed kidney recovery,
highlighting the importance of early and sustained BP optimization. These findings
support the use of age-appropriate BP targets, ideally informed by physiologic norms, to
guide hemodynamic management in children at risk for or with AKI.

adult evidence, with limited direct pediatric data. The only pediatric RCT is the Goal-

(GDP-AKIp) trial, which demonstrated that goal-directed perfusion maintaining indexed
oxygen delivery ≥360 ml/min/m² during cardiopulmonary bypass reduced AKI from
42.2% to 28.1% in children undergoing cardiac surgery.389 No pediatric-specific MAP
targets exist. Elevated central venous pressure is associated with worse kidney function in
children with congenital cardiac disease, with eGFR declining acutely as central venous

a recent propensity-matched study found norepinephrine was associated with lower
mortality compared to epinephrine without clear kidney benefit.509 Renal near-infrared

3.12.2 Nutritional intervention in AKI
Practice Point 3.12.2.1.: In children with AKI or AKD, age-recommended protein
targets can be used without protein restriction, and a minimum daily protein intake
R
of 2.5 grams/kg should be maintained for children receiving CRRT.
Protein-energy malnutrition is common in critically ill children with AKI and is
associated with adverse outcomes. Children receiving CRRT are at particularly high risk
dueD to increased protein requirements for growth and additional protein losses through the
extracorporeal circuit. Importantly, there is no evidence that protein intake at age-
recommended protein levels delays kidney recovery in children with AKI, and restriction
may contribute to negative nitrogen balance and worse outcomes. In a large retrospective
study of 511 critically ill children, higher protein intake (≥80% of estimated needs) was
not associated with delayed resolution of AKI, even after adjustment for severity of
illness.481
Children receiving CRRT require even higher protein intake to counteract
extracorporeal losses. A prospective study of 41 children receiving CRRT found that
median protein delivery was 2.0 g/kg/day, with a substantial proportion failing to meet
institutional protein goals of 2.5 g/kg/day. Children who received only enteral nutrition
met protein goals only 13% of the time, while those on combined parenteral and enteral
support met goals 60% of the time. Transitioning from parenteral to enteral nutrition was
identified as a high-risk period for inadequate protein delivery.513 These findings
underscore the importance of maintaining at least 2.5 g/kg/day protein intake during
CRRT, with consideration of supplemental parenteral nutrition or protein additives when
enteral nutrition alone is insufficient.

Practice Point 3.12.2.2: Supplement water-soluble vitamins routinely in children
receiving CRRT.
Compared with adults, infants and young children have lower baseline reserves of
many water-soluble vitamins and higher weight-adjusted metabolic requirements,
increasing the risk of clinically significant deficiency during prolonged RRT.
Observational data consistently demonstrate reductions in circulating concentrations of
several water-soluble vitamins during CRRT, and deficiency may develop rapidly in the
absence of supplementation.514 Deficiency of water-soluble vitamins in critically ill
T
children may contribute to impaired energy metabolism, lactic acidosis (thiamine
deficiency), compromised immune and antioxidant function (vitamin C deficiency),
neurologic dysfunction, cytopenias, and poor wound healing. Water-soluble vitamins
have a wide therapeutic index, and the risk of toxicity with appropriate supplementation is
F
low, as excess amounts are generally cleared.
Supplementation should be initiated at the start of CRRT and continued for the
duration of therapy. Dosing should account for age, weight, nutritional intake (enteral or
A
parenteral), and CRRT intensity. In some cases, higher-than-standard daily recommended
doses may be required to offset extracorporeal losses, particularly in infants, small
children, or those receiving high effluent rates.

3.12.3 Hepatorenal syndrome-associated AKI
Practice Point 3.12.3.1: It is reasonable to use terlipressin or norepinephrine for
children with hepatorenal syndrome-associated AKI depending on the clinical care
context and patient condition.
D
Pediatric hepatorenal syndrome physiology is similar to adult physiology but data
on terlipressin in children are limited. However, retrospective data suggests that
terlipressin may be effective in reversal of the hepatorenal pathology in children and is
safe to use in children when administered as an infusion in the ICU, as intermittent dosing
is associated with a higher incidence of gut ischemia.
Investigators retrospectively reviewed 16 pediatric participants with liver disease
and AKI who received terlipressin in a single center in the UK.515 Terlipressin
administration led to a sustained increase in MAP and improvement in kidney parameters,
particularly in participants with hepatorenal syndrome. Notably, terlipressin was
associated with improvements in SCr and urine output, especially in children with
hepatorenal syndrome (71.4% response rate at 48 hours). Adverse effects were reported
as 3 instances of digital ischemia and feeding intolerance. Although 47.5% of participants
died (28.5% in hepatorenal syndrome-associated AKI subgroup), mortality was attributed
to underlying disease severity and not terlipressin use. Larger prospective trials are
needed to confirm the efficacy and assess the role of terlipressin as a bridge to
transplantation.
187

Data on the role of norepinephrine as an alternative is absent for children. In
children, evidence supporting vasoconstrictor therapy is sparse and largely derived from
observational studies, small case series, and extrapolation from adult data. Nevertheless,
the underlying pathophysiology of hepatorenal syndrome-associated AKI is similar across
age groups, and potential benefits in kidney perfusion and function are biologically
plausible. The Work Group judged that a cautious suggestion for use of terlipressin in
children is appropriate, with emphasis on individualized decision-making and careful
monitoring.

3.12.4 Theophylline use in neonates
Recommendation 3.12.4.1: We recommend a single dose of theophylline be given to
neonates with severe perinatal asphyxia for the prevention of AKI (1A).
F
This recommendation places a high value on the reduction of AKI incidence in neonates
with severe perinatal asphyxia with a single dose of theophylline, given the consistent
short-term benefits and low risk of harm. However, the long-term kidney and
neurodevelopmental safety of this intervention remains incompletely understood, and
A
most studies to date were conducted in settings without therapeutic hypothermia.
Therefore, centers using therapeutic hypothermia should consider the lack of interaction
data when implementing this recommendation.
R
Key information
Balance of benefits and harms
Neonates with severe perinatal asphyxia have a high risk of AKI. Renal
hypoperfusion and ischemia–reperfusion injury are central mechanisms in this population.
AdeDnosine-mediated renal vasoconstriction contributes to reduced glomerular filtration in
the early post-asphyxia period. Theophylline, a nonselective adenosine receptor
antagonist, may attenuate this vasoconstriction and improve kidney blood flow when
administered shortly after birth.
A single early dose of theophylline has been shown to reduce the incidence and
severity of AKI in multiple RCTs. Meta-analysis of 5 RCTs comparing theophylline
versus no theophylline in a total of 356 neonates with perinatal asphyxia showed that
children treated with theophylline had a significantly lower incidence of AKI (OR: 0.24;
95% CI: 0.16–0.36). There was no difference in mortality (OR: 0.86; 95% CI: 0.46–1.62;
Supplementary Table S62).516
Theophylline has a narrow therapeutic index and is associated with potential
adverse effects, including cardiac arrhythmias, gastrointestinal disturbances, central
nervous system irritability, and potential drug interactions. However, in published
neonatal studies using a single prophylactic dose, serious adverse events have been
uncommon, and most reported side effects were transient and self-limited. The risk of
toxicity may be increased in preterm infants or in settings without appropriate dosing
188

accuracy and monitoring capacity. However, long-term neurodevelopmental outcomes
and safety in combination with therapeutic hypothermia remain understudied.
Certainty of evidence
There is high certainty of evidence that, for neonates with perinatal asphyxia,
theophylline greatly reduces the risk of AKI but may not affect risk of death, however,
with no evidence regarding serious adverse events among studies reporting AKI-related
outcomes.516
T
Values and preferences
This recommendation places a high value on the prevention of AKI in neonates
with severe perinatal asphyxia, given its association with increased morbidity, mortality,
and long-term kidney risk. Healthcare professionals and caregivers are likely to prioritize
F
short-term improvements in kidney function and fluid balance, particularly when the
intervention is simple, inexpensive, and administered as a single dose. In settings with
limited capacity for close cardiorespiratory monitoring, healthcare professionals may
place greater weight on potential harms. Conversely, in settings where RRT is unavailable
A
or difficult to provide, the value placed on AKI prevention may be particularly high.
Overall, the Work Group considered that most informed parents and healthcare
professionals would favor administration of a single prophylactic dose of theophylline in
R
neonates with severe perinatal asphyxia to reduce the risk of AKI, given the seriousness
of the outcome and the generally low incidence of significant short-term adverse effects
when appropriately dosed.
ResDource use and costs
The administration of a single dose of intravenous theophylline is a low-cost
intervention that requires minimal additional resources. Theophylline is available in both
low- and high-resource settings and is inexpensive. No specialized equipment or
monitoring beyond standard neonatal intensive care practices is needed. Because the
intervention is administered as a one-time dose within the first hour of life, it does not add
substantial labor or complexity to neonatal resuscitation or early postnatal care.
Importantly, the potential to prevent AKI may reduce downstream healthcare utilization.
As such, the use of prophylactic theophylline in eligible neonates is likely to be cost-
saving or cost-neutral from a healthcare system perspective, although this has not been
specifically studied.
Considerations for implementation
Successful implementation of this recommendation requires timely identification
of neonates with severe perinatal asphyxia and early administration of theophylline. This
necessitates clear local protocols for recognizing eligibility based on criteria such as
Apgar scores, cord pH, base deficit, and need for resuscitation. Standardization of dosing
and incorporation into neonatal resuscitation or post-resuscitation order sets may facilitate
uptake. In settings where therapeutic hypothermia is used, additional caution is warranted,
189

as data on the combined use of theophylline and cooling are limited. Even in resource-
limited settings, implementation is feasible because the intervention involves a single
dose and relies primarily on monitoring that is already standard for critically ill neonates.
Rationale
Neonates with severe perinatal asphyxia are at high risk for AKI due to renal
hypoperfusion and ischemia–reperfusion injury. Clinical trials in neonates have
demonstrated that a single prophylactic dose of theophylline administered shortly after
birth reduces the incidence of AKI, lowers peak SCr, improves urine output, and may
T
reduce the need for RRT.
Although the number of RCTs is limited and most studies are relatively small, the
evidence consistently demonstrates kidney benefit with minimal harm. The magnitude of
F
benefit is particularly meaningful in settings where access to RRT is limited or
unavailable. The intervention is relatively simple, involving a single weight-based
intravenous dose, which limits cumulative drug exposure and toxicity risk. Reported
adverse effects in neonatal trials are generally mild and self-limited. Serious adverse
A
events are rare when dosing is appropriate.
The Work Group judged that the benefits clearly outweigh the potential harms,
supporting a Level 1 recommendation for use, with careful weight-based dosing and
R
monitoring.
3.12.5 Use of nitric oxide in children
Recommendation 3.12.5.1: We recommend against routine use of inhaled nitric
oxidDe to prevent cardiac surgery-associated AKI in children with low-complexity
cardiac lesions and no signs of pulmonary hypertension or right ventricular
dysfunction undergoing cardiopulmonary bypass (1C).
This recommendation applies specifically to children undergoing elective, low-complexity
congenital heart surgery with cardiopulmonary bypass who do not have significant
pulmonary hypertension or right ventricular dysfunction, in whom inhaled nitric oxide
did not reduce AKI or RRT in a large RCT. The Work Group judged that routine use of
inhaled nitric oxide in this setting is not recommended as it does not prevent AKI and may
introduce risks, cost, and unnecessary complexity without clear clinical advantages.
Inhaled nitric oxide may still be indicated for other cardiopulmonary indications in select
populations.
Key information
Balance of benefits and harms
AKI is a significant complication following cardiopulmonary bypass in
children.517 Inhaled nitric oxide has been proposed to reduce the risk of AKI by
enhancing renal perfusion; reducing ischemia–reperfusion injury, oxidative stress, and
hemolysis; preserving microvascular integrity; and attenuating inflammation. However,
190

there is no evidence supporting the clinical efficacy of inhaled nitric oxide for preventing
AKI in low-complexity pediatric cardiac surgeries.
A systematic review of 6 RCTs among children undergoing surgery with
cardiopulmonary bypass found that inhaled nitric oxide versus placebo had no effect on
mortality (5 trials, 1686 participants; RR: 1.11; 95% CI: 0.53–2.34), development of AKI
(1 trial, 1364 participants; RR: 1.16; 95% CI: 0.88–1.53), or use of RRT (1 trial, 1364
participants; RR: 0.94; 95% CI: 0.71–1.25; Supplementary Table S63).518-524
T
Only one large, multicenter RCT has examined AKI in infants undergoing
congenital heart surgery and nitric oxide use. The Nitric Oxide During Cardiopulmonary
Bypass to Improve Recovery in Infants With Congenital Heart Defects trial (NITRIC)
was a large, multicenter, double-blind RCT conducted across 6 pediatric cardiac centers
F
in Australia, New Zealand, and the Netherlands.524 The trial enrolled 1371 children under
2 years of age undergoing elective, low-complexity congenital heart surgery with
cardiopulmonary bypass, comparing inhaled nitric oxide at 20 ppm delivered into the
cardiopulmonary bypass oxygenator with standard care. The primary outcome (ventilator-
A
free days at 28 days) did not differ significantly between groups (median 26.6 days vs.
26.4 days; P=0.92). There were no significant differences in secondary outcomes,
including the composite of low cardiac output syndrome, extracorporeal life support, or
death by day 28. Rates of postoperative AKI and RRT were also not significantly
R
different. These findings suggest that inhaled nitric oxide administration during
cardiopulmonary bypass does not improve clinical outcomes or reduce AKI incidence in
infants undergoing low-complexity congenital heart surgery.
DIn one small RCT of 16 children undergoing cardiopulmonary bypass surgery,

0.9 vs. 3.25 ml ± 1.0 total within 48 hours; p<0.05) and less positive postoperative fluid
balance (187±122 vs. 317±271).518
Some evidence also suggests that inhaled nitric oxide may increase the risk of
AKI, particularly in children with acute lung injury or acute respiratory distress
syndrome. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 10 RCTs involving 1363
participants found that inhaled nitric oxide increased AKI risk (RR: 1.40; 95% CI: 1.06–
1.83; p=0.02), with a dose-dependent effect, as higher cumulative doses of inhaled nitric
oxide significantly increased AKI risk (RR: 1.52; p=0.004). However, this harm was
specific to participants with acute respiratory distress syndrome and was not observed in
non-acute respiratory distress syndrome populations.525 A 2016 Cochrane review, which
examined 4 studies, found that abnormal kidney function was more frequent in the
inhaled nitric oxide group compared to the control group, with a relative risk of 1.59
(95% CI: 1.17–2.16, I² = 0%).526
While inhaled nitric oxide is generally well tolerated when used short-term, there
are potential harms and adverse effects, including methemoglobinemia, nitrogen dioxide
191

toxicity, rebound pulmonary hypertension, systemic hypotension, platelet dysfunction,
and bleeding. These harms may negate any benefit when used for AKI prevention in the
absence of pulmonary hypertension.
The Work Group judged that the balance of benefits and harms of inhaled nitric
oxide for the specific purpose of AKI prevention is context-dependent. In children with
clear pulmonary hypertension or right ventricular dysfunction, inhaled nitric oxide may
provide cardiopulmonary benefit that justifies potential risks, with kidney protection as a
possible secondary advantage. However, when used solely for kidney protection in
T
hemodynamically stable children without pulmonary vascular disease, the anticipated
benefits are uncertain.

Values and preferences
Most families and healthcare professionals place a high value on avoiding
unnecessary interventions in children undergoing low-complexity cardiac surgery,
R
particularly when the anticipated benefit is uncertain and the therapy involves additional
cost, monitoring, and potential risks. Parents and caregivers are likely to value avoidance
of unnecessary medications, potential side effects, and additional interventions. The
principle of minimizing nonessential therapies in children is often strongly supported in
shaDred decision-making discussions.
Many healthcare professionals would prioritize minimizing exposure to therapies
that do not demonstrate clear and consistent benefit on patient-important outcomes. Some
variability in preferences may exist, and some healthcare professionals may favor broader
use of inhaled nitric oxide, based on institutional experience, physiologic rationale, or
perceived safety. However, in the absence of compelling evidence of benefit in children
at low risk, most informed healthcare professionals would likely favor a conservative
approach.
Resource use and costs
Inhaled nitric oxide is a high-cost intervention that requires specialized delivery
systems, continuous monitoring, and trained personnel for safe administration. The
infrastructure needed to deliver inhaled nitric oxide, whether integrated into the
cardiopulmonary bypass circuit or postoperatively in the ICU, may not be readily
available in all settings, particularly in low- and middle-resource settings. In the absence
of demonstrated benefit for AKI prevention in low-risk pediatric cardiac surgical
populations, routine use of inhaled nitric oxide represents a poor allocation of resources.
192

Its continued use in this context may divert financial and staffing capacity from
interventions with stronger evidence of clinical efficacy.
Considerations for implementation
This recommendation applies specifically to children undergoing elective, low-
complexity congenital heart surgery with cardiopulmonary bypass, such as ventricular
septal defect or atrial septal defect repairs, who do not have significant pulmonary
hypertension or ventricular dysfunction. In these children, inhaled nitric oxide should not
be used solely for the purpose of AKI prevention. However, inhaled nitric oxide may still
T
be indicated for other clinical reasons. It is important that healthcare professionals do not
generalize this recommendation to higher-risk populations, such as those with single-
ventricle physiology, complex cyanotic heart lesions, or preexisting pulmonary vascular
disease, where the benefit-risk profile may differ.
F
Rationale
Cardiac surgery–associated AKI is related to ischemia–reperfusion injury,
inflammation, hemolysis, hemodynamic instability, and venous congestion during and
A
after cardiopulmonary bypass. Inhaled nitric oxide has been proposed as a preventive
strategy based on its potential to improve right ventricular performance, enhance
pulmonary vascular function, mitigate hemolysis-mediated nitric oxide depletion, and
attenuate oxidative stress.
R
Available clinical studies evaluating inhaled nitric oxide for AKI prevention in
pediatric cardiac surgery are limited, heterogeneous, and largely conducted in mixed or
higher-risk populations. Evidence does not consistently demonstrate a meaningful
reduDction in patient-important outcomes such as need for RRT, prolonged ICU stay, or
mortality. Furthermore, inhaled nitric oxide therapy carries potential risks and burdens,
including methemoglobinemia, nitrogen dioxide exposure, rebound pulmonary
hypertension with abrupt discontinuation, platelet inhibition, and potential contribution to
postoperative bleeding. Although these harms are generally uncommon and manageable,
their relevance increases when expected benefit is small or uncertain.
Accordingly, the recommendation against routine use reflects the low baseline
risk of severe AKI, lack of evidence of clinically meaningful benefit, and the additional
risks and resource implications associated with inhaled nitric oxide therapy in this setting.
