# Chapter 4: Drug- and Nephrotoxin-Associated AKI

AKI
Drug-associated kidney disease is a significant problem in community- and hospital-
based care. Approximately one-fourth of all medications used in hospitals can cause injury to
the kidneys, and these substances contribute to approximately one-fourth of all AKI episodes.
There are multiple mechanisms by which drugs can cause AKI or AKD, although not all
involve structural damage to the renal tubules or other parenchymal components.
Medications like renin-angiotensin system inhibitors (RASiT) and sodium glucose transporter
2 inhibitors (SGLT2i) may cause a rise in SCr through hemodynamic effects on GFR but
confer proven and clinically significant outcome-modifying benefits for high-risk patient
populations, including those with heart failure, CKD, and diabetes.
F
Iodinated contrast agents have long been associated with AKI, but their direct effect
on kidney function and nephrotoxicity remains debated. Meanwhile, the types of contrast
media and clinical practices to increase the safety of radiocontrast exposure have changed
A
considerably over time.
This chapter provides guidance on drug stewardship and contrast use in people at risk
of or with AKI or AKD. Throughout this chapter, the terms "nephrotoxic" and
"nephrotoxicity" aRre used only to describe medications or chemicals when they cause
structural damage to the kidneys. This is intended to makes clear a distinction between drug
effects on GFR due to alterations in kidney hemodynamics, from those that induce structural
kidney damage.

c) Dysfunction without damage: This category refers to the effects of drugs through
systematic hypoperfusion and/or a decline in the intraglomerular pressure that lead to
a decrease Rin GFR and increase in SCr without structural damage. Examples are
ACEi, ARBs, and SGLT2i. Notably, the acute reduction in GFR can be anticipated

people with CKD and proteinuria.
D
d) Dysfunction with damage: These include many drugs exerting more than one effect of
structural damage or prolonged damage leading to reduced GFR. Examples include
nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), calcineurin inhibitors,
aminoglycosides, and amphotericin B.
Of relevance to clinical decision making, some classes of drugs can cause an acute
reduction in GFR but confer important long-term kidney and cardiovascular health benefits
(e.g., ACEi, ARB, and SGLT2i). This is particularly relevant to management of people with
heart failure and CKD, who may have a rise in SCr meeting criteria for AKI or AKD
following initiation or dose escalation of such medications. Small declines in eGFR in such
situations are expected and have been associated with better long-term outcomes. Similarly,
diuretics for acute heart failure may cause hemoconcentration and changes in SCr
concentration meeting AKI criteria. However, achieving decongestion in this situation is
crucial for improving both short- and long-term outcomes.

4.2. Kidney protective strategies
Recommendation 4.2.1: For children or adults with or at risk of AKI, we recommend
drug and nephrotoxin stewardship using a multimodal approach (1C).
This recommendation places high value on patient safety by seeking to minimize the risk of
adverse reactions to drugs eliminated by the kidney and by reducing the incidence and
severity of AKI related to drug exposures. It emphasizes the principles of drug stewardship
which refers to the use of multiple strategies to promote responsible, effective, and safe use of
medicines to ensure they are initiated when needed at the right dose, monitored and dose
T
adjusted as needed to prevent or minimize harm, and are discontinued when no longer
beneficial to a person’s health. A clinical pharmacist often leads this form of multimodal
stewardship, but other healthcare professionals may take on this role. This recommendation
places a lower value on the additional resource use associated with the multimodal drug
F
stewardship. Although the certainty of evidence for a multimodal strategy is low (or very
low), the Work Group felt that a Level 1 recommendation was warranted given the potential
for harm and cost reduction in applicable populations.
A
Key information
Balance of benefits and harms
Drug and nephrotoxin stewardship strategies offer the benefits of safer and more
effective use of medications. Potential harms include avoidance of otherwise effective
R
medications for the treatment of acute and chronic illnesses due to fears of causing AKI.
Other challenges are differences in financial costs between different drugs with different
nephrotoxic potential. The balance between risk and benefit comes into consideration when
prescribing potentially nephrotoxic medications or medications cleared by the kidney and
decDiding when benefits of treatment exceed the risk of causing or worsening AKI, and
whether it is appropriate to initiate, dose-adjust, or discontinue such medications. Drug
stewardship can help support these decisions by improving recognition of potential
medication harms and involving multidisciplinary healthcare professionals in medication
stewardship decisions to balance protection of kidney health while ensuring adequate
treatment of underlying diseases with acceptable levels of AKI risk.
Vancomycin has been the focus of several studies. A review of 9 studies (1 RCT and
8 nonrandomized controlled studies), including a total of 1973 adults showed that
vancomycin administered via continuous infusion was associated with a significantly lower
risk of AKI (OR: 0.47; 95% CI: 0.34–0.64) compared to intermittent dosing but there was no
difference in mortality (Supplementary Table S64).534-536
A systematic review of 12 nonrandomized controlled studies including 7548 adults
concluded that vancomycin dosing based on AUC was associated with a significantly lower
risk of AKI development compared to trough-only guided dosing (RR: 0.68; 95% CI: 0.59–
0.77; Supplementary Table S65).537

D
investigated in children and adults. As mentioned earlier, pharmacist interventions based on
vancomycin monitoring reduced the risk of AKI in children and adults.538, 539 A single RCT

Overall, these studies demonstrate that drug stewardship including therapeutic drug
monitoring and informed interventions can prevent drug induced AKI. However, additional
resources (laboratory, experts, costs) are needed. Also, in some situations, administration of
nephrotoxic drugs may be needed to treat the underlying illness. Importantly, drug
stewardship is only 1 strategy to protect kidney health and may not be sufficient to prevent
multi-factorial AKI.

Certainty of evidence
There is moderate certainty of evidence that continuous versus intermittent infusion of
vancomycin reduces the risk of AKI in adults but does not affect mortality. The certainty was
downgraded for some methodological limitations and sparseness of data.534-536 There were no
eligible studies comparing vancomycin infusion in children.
There is low certainty of evidence that AUC dosing of vancomycin versus trough-
based dosing reduces the risk of AKI in adults but does not affect mortality (with very low
certainty of evidence). The certainty was downgraded for methodological limitations of
T
observational studies and, for mortality, sparseness of data.537 There were no eligible studies
comparing vancomycin infusion in children.
There is low certainty of evidence regarding CDSS interventions (including
F
therapeutic monitoring) for vancomycin in adults. The overall certainty of evidence was
based on prioritizing incident AKI, but there is moderate certainty of evidence for a possible
increase in RRT and mostly moderate to high certainty of evidence for mortality and other
sequelae of AKI.151, 547 The certainty of evidence for incident AKI was reduced due to
A
sparseness of data (adequately powered single study). The certainty of evidence for RRT was
downgraded for imprecision. There were no eligible studies of CDSS interventions for

For adults with AKI, with low certainty of evidence, pharmacist involvement in
vancomycin dosing is associated with reduced risk of death and incident AKI. The certainty
of evidence was downgraded for methodological quality (almost all data come from
observational studies).538, 539 From a subset of these studies, there is very low certainty of
evidDence of a similar effect in children; however, the certainty of evidence for children was
also downgraded for inconsistency and imprecision.
Antibiotic monitoring (other than for vancomycin) in adults with AKI has low

limitations and sparseness of data.546 There were no eligible studies of pharmacist monitoring
of all drugs in children.

Values and preferences
Drug and nephrotoxin stewardship are predicated on the understanding that there are
no specific treatments available for AKI and that patients and healthcare professionals prefer
prevention. This recommendation values diligent monitoring through stewardship efforts to
avoid thinking that drug-related kidney disease is an accepted risk for all. It is a preference
towards an active approach to patient-centered care designed to avoid medication-related
harm.
Resource use and costs
T
Available resources are an important consideration in the selection of stewardship
strategies and require that institutions commit resources to medication safety (Table 28).548
Resources to accomplish drug and nephrotoxin stewardship include support for healthcare
professionals committed and assigned to providing the set of coordinated patient care
F
management strategies. When deciding the cost of personnel, it is important to consider the
immediate and long-term cost of drug-related acute kidney diseases. The additional costs of
dedicating personnel and resources to support drug stewardship could be offset by reduced
total drug costs, shorter lengths of stay in ICU and hospital, and improved downstream health
A
status by avoiding AKI.549

Rationale
There are multiple approaches to prevent the development or progression of drug- or
nephrotoxin-related AKD or adverse events from drugs eliminated by the kidney. As no
single strategy has demonstrated superiority over others, the best option for surveillance and
mitigation of drug related adverse events in people of all ages is to employ a set of multiple,
coordinated management strategies (multimodal approach) in people at risk for these
complications.550 Selecting a less nephrotoxic drug in people at-risk for AKI, reducing
nephrotoxin burden, or applying dose reduction/trough reduction to minimize exposure as
part of a stewardship strategy can result in improved clinical outcomes and reduce drug use
T
and related costs. Therapeutic drug monitoring, and surveillance for drug-drug interactions
have also been included in effective interventions. Table 31 of the KDIGO 2024 Clinical
Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of CKD provides suggestions for
non-nephrotoxic alternatives to nephrotoxic drugs.17 These alternatives can also be applied to
F
people with AKI or AKD. The impact of medication burden and the principles of medication
reviews and reconciliation to ensure medication safety that are outlined in Figure 46 of the
KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of CKD also
apply to people with AKI or AKD.17 However, in the context of AKI and potentially
A
fluctuating GFR, closer monitoring and more frequent adjustments may be required.
Practice Point 4.2.1: Strategies to manage drug-related acute kidney disease include:
d) discontinuation of offending nephrotoxic agents known to cause kidney damage,
R
if possible;
e) provision of a therapeutic equivalent alternative that is less nephrotoxic, if
available and necessary;
f) reduction in dose if drug continuation is necessary for those drugs with a dose
Ddependent direct nephrotoxic effect.
An assessment of risk versus benefit is required when determining whether to
discontinue or continue medications in situations where ongoing exposure can possibly lead
to development or progression of AKI. This decision should be made with consideration of
the availability of therapeutically equivalent, alternative agents, and the relative effectiveness
of alternative agents for the clinical condition the drug is being prescribed for. Another factor
to consider is whether to opt for dose reduction versus discontinuation which may be affected
by the mechanism of nephrotoxicity. If toxicity is dose dependent, a dose reduction may be
beneficial as opposed to dose-independent toxicity (e.g.,, in drug-induced acute interstitial
nephritis) where drug discontinuation is required.
Practice Point 4.2.2: Therapeutic drug monitoring is a component of multimodal
stewardship that involves serial assessments of drug concentrations in children and
adults receiving potentially nephrotoxic drugs, drugs excreted by the kidneys, and
drugs with narrow therapeutic margins or low therapeutic indices.
Therapeutic drug monitoring is the clinical practice of measuring plasma
concentrations of specific drugs at designated intervals to optimize drug dosing. It can be
202

used to assess drugs with narrow therapeutic indices that cause nephrotoxicity, are eliminated
by the kidneys, or both. (Table 29).551, 552 Therapeutic drug monitoring is appropriate for
potentially nephrotoxic drugs that require vigilant monitoring to prevent AKI development or
progression.553 An appropriately trained member of the care team or a CDSS is needed to
interpret therapeutic drug monitoring results and make dosage adjustments.551 Follow-up
monitoring after drug discontinuation may be needed if supratherapeutic drug concentrations
are observed.554 Importantly, there is a strong association between nonadherence to
therapeutic drug monitoring guidelines for antibiotics and the development of
nephrotoxicity.555 Therapeutic drug monitoring should also be considered for classes of
T
medications that depend on renal clearance and may accumulate in case of reduced kidney

Practice Point 4.2.3: Surveillance for pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic drug-
drug interactions that increase the risk of AKI is a component of multimodal
stewardship.
There are several pharmacokinetic drug-drug interactions that potentiate the
likelihood of developing AKI (Table 30).556 Some of these interactions can be avoided by
selecting alternative drugs that do not alter the metabolism of another drug (i.e. selecting
azithromycin instead of erythromycin and clarithromycin to reduce the risk of
rhabdomyolysis when coadministration with a statin is necessary).
Also, several pharmacodynamic drug combinations can result in an increased risk of
developing AKI, for instance the coadministration of NSAIDs, diuretics, and RASi.556, 557
While it may be necessary to continue coadministration of drugs such as ACEi and diuretics
for people with congestive heart failure, coadministration of NSAIDS can often be avoided.
203

The likelihood of developing AKI increases depending on the number of nephrotoxin
exposures for an individual. Nephrotoxic burden refers to the cumulative or aggregate
exposure to nephrotoxins, with consideration to the nephrotoxic potential for each drug,
evaluated within a relevant time frame.550, 558-560 Specifically, each additional nephrotoxin has
been estimated to contribute to a 1.2–1.8-fold increase in odds of AKI per drug in children
and a 1.2–3.2-fold increase in odds per drug in adults.561 Calculations for assessing
nephrotoxic burden have been proposed.558, 562, 563 Surveillance for the nephrotoxic burden of
≥3 drugs with notification to the healthcare professional using CDSS has been shown to
reduce AKI incidence and severity in children.420, 564, 565

4.5. Management of drugs eliminated by the kidneys
Practice Point 4.5.1: Dosing of drugs excreted by the kidneys should not be based on
creatinine clearance or glomerular filtration rate (GFR) estimated using equations that
assume a steady state of creatinine production and clearance in children and adults
children with developing or resolving AKI or AKD.
D
Practice Point 4.5.2: Trends in SCr and urine output should be used to guide dosing of
drugs excreted by the kidney. In adults with AKI, calculation of a KeGFR may be
useful when sufficient SCr measurements are available.
Ideal methods for measuring GFR use urinary clearance of exogenous compounds
such as inulin or iohexol but these approaches are not practical to guide drug dosing in
routine clinical practice due to costs and technical challenges. Measurement of urinary
creatinine clearance with timed urine collections is an option to estimate GFR but is limited
by overestimation of true GFR by 10%–20% due to tubular secretion of creatinine and
inaccuracies in timing of collections.
The Work Group agrees with the following practice point related to drug dosing from
the KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of CKD
and believes it also applies to people with AKI and AKD.
212

Practice Point 4.5.3: Consider and adapt drug dosing in people where GFR, non-GFR
determinants of the filtration markers, or volume of distribution are not in a steady
state.
In the context of AKI, it is important to recognize that GFR estimating equations such
as the Cockcroft-Gault, MDRD, and CKD-EPI equations were developed and validated for
people with CKD and assume a steady state of SCr.586, 587 They will overestimate a person's
GFR when SCr is rising (potentially resulting in drug overdosing) and underestimate GFR
when SCr is falling (potentially resulting in drug underdosing). For people with AKI, it is
T
important to consider trends in SCr and urine output when dosing drugs excreted by the
kidney. Initial dosing may start with a standard dose based on a person's eGFR before the
AKI episode, or a dose that would be appropriate for severely abnormal kidney function,
depending on the clinical situation. A person who is anuric should be considered to have a
F
GFR <10 ml/min.588 Changes in SCr and urine output along with therapeutic drug monitoring
if available, can be used to guide and adjust subsequent doses.
KeGFR accounts for the rate of change in SCr over time, providing a more accurate
A
real-time estimate of GFR that can be used to guide drug doses with changing SCr values
accompanying AKI.589 In 1 study, use of a KeGFR equation resulted in changes in drug
dosing in approximately 20% of participants compared to non-kinetic eGFR equations.590 At
this time, no one equation can be endorsed over another and all have limitations.574, 587

4.7. Contrast-associated AKI (CA-AKI): RecFognition
Practice Point 4.7.1: Consider the differential diagnosis of post-contrast AKI, which
may have causes other than contrast exposure.
A
Contrast-associated AKI (CA-AKI) refers to AKI following parenteral administration
of iodinated radiocontrast media. The reported incidence of CA-AKI has varied in the
literature with use of different definitions, with some reviews reporting it as the third most
common cause of AKI in hospitalized adults. The attribution of contrast exposure to AKI
continues to be a sRource of debate; however, CA-AKI has been consistently associated with
adverse short- and long-term outcomes.
The Work Group agrees with the suggestion from Section 4.4 of the KDIGO 2024
Clinical Practice Guideline for the Evaluation and Management of CKD that the term
D
“contrast-associated AKI” be used to recognize the broader differential diagnosis for the
etiology of AKI in many such cases, which may or may not be solely attributable to
contrast.17 The presumption that all AKI following contrast administration is CA-AKI has led
to a lack of specificity in case definitions and associated epidemiology; indeed, some have
questioned the existence and clinical importance of CA-AKI.603 Nonetheless, in 2023, the
American College of Radiology (ACR) Contrast Manual stated the position of the ACR
Committee on Drugs and Contrast Media that CA-AKI is a real, albeit rare, entity.604 The
Work Group concurred with this statement while recognizing that CA-AKI includes other
etiologies of AKI following radiocontrast exposure, including multifactorial causes
(combined with radiocontrast).

reported a pooled RR of 0.80 (95% CI: 0.61–1.04). Subgroup analyses of trials restricted to

participants, used as a surrogate of trial quality (RR: 0.93; 95% CI: 0.66–1.30).

Volume depletion is a risk factor for CA-AKI. Accordingly, the prophylactic
administration of intravenous crystalloids to avoid hypovolemia in people at high risk of CA-
AKI has been a standard practice for decades. However, the clinical significance of small
acute changes in SCr that define CA-AKI has been questioned in recent years. This has led to
reassessment of the benefits versus harms of empiric prophylactic volume expansion to
prevent CA-AKI, even in higher risk groups. Furthermore, the growing literature
demonstrating that volume overload and congestion can cause or worsen AKI (in addition to
potentially causing acute pulmonary edema) has also raised awareness of the potential
importance of personalized fluid management in this setting. Recent evidence suggests that
T

CA-AKI by individualizing fluid management.

Values and preferences
People at high risk of CA-AKI and healthcare professionals generally value
interventions that protect kidney function without introducing significant additional risk or
burden. Strategies that are simple, evidence-based, and have a favorable safety profile are
preferred. Prophylactic periprocedural volume expansion with intravenous crystalloids is
noninvasive, low cost, and well established in clinical practice. Preferences often lean toward
T
0.9% saline due to its widespread availability, lower cost, familiarity, and relative simplicity
of preparation and use. People at high-risk and their healthcare professionals typically
prioritize kidney protection and accept the inconvenience of fluid administration if it can
meaningfully reduce the risk of CA-AKI. However, people with conditions like heart failure
F
may place a high value on avoiding volume overload and may prefer tailored or more
conservative hydration approaches. For people with or at risk of complications from volume
overload, shared decision-making is important, with preferences influenced by perceived
risk, severity of comorbidities, prioAr experiences, and trust in the recommendation of the
clinical team.
Resource use and costs
Prophylactic intravenous crystalloids for people at high risk of CA-AKI is generally
R
considered a low-cost intervention, especially when compared to the potential expenses
associated with managing CA-AKI, including prolonged hospitalization, dialysis, and long-
term care for CKD. The direct costs involve the solutions themselves, intravenous
administration supplies, and healthcare professional time for monitoring and delivery. Saline
D
(0.9%) is inexpensive, widely available, and already integrated into most hospital formularies
and workflows, making it a low-cost and low-resource option. In contrast, isotonic sodium
bicarbonate is generally more expensive and may require additional resources to support on-
site compounding or special preparation. This can increase pharmacy and nursing workload
and resource requirements, as well as the potential for error.
Indirect costs may arise from extended pre- or post-procedure observation,
particularly in outpatient settings where additional staffing or bed occupancy is required.
While the intervention is relatively inexpensive, the cost-effectiveness depends on
appropriate patient selection. Targeting those at high risk of CA-AKI can avoid unnecessary
resource use in people at low risk. In settings with limited capacity or staffing, such as
outpatient settings, the logistics of administering fluids pre- and post-contrast may also
contribute to operational strain. Overall, when used judiciously in people at high risk,
prophylactic intravenous fluid administration is a resource-efficient strategy that may prevent
costlier complications.

Considerations for implementation
Implementing this recommendation requires coordination across clinical teams to
identify people at high risk, and standardized protocols and infrastructure to support timely
fluid administration. Key considerations include use of strategies for identifying people at
high-risk using risk assessment tools. Protocols should promote the use of 0.9% saline, with
guidance on rate and duration of fluid administration with adjustments for comorbidities like
heart failure where fluid overload is a concern. Decision support tools that are integrated into
clinical workflows in radiology, cardiology, and emergency departments may aid
implementation.153 Education and training for staff, accompanied by audit and feedback, can
T

outpatient procedures. Overall, successful implementation depends on institutional
commitment, interdisciplinary collaboration, and mechanisms to monitor adherence and

Rationale
The use of iodinated radiocontrast media has been associated with the occurrence of
AKI, with varying rates reported deApending on the population studied, the type, route and
dose of contrast agent being used, and the presence of other risk factors for AKI. Existing
data support the use of periprocedural fluid administration in people at high risk without fluid
overload but there is no evidence that a particular type of fluid is superior for AKI prevention
to 0.9% saline which is available worldwide. This recommendation is aligned with the joint
R
ACR/NKF Guideline.609 This recommendation is specific to fluid administration for CA-AKI
prevention, and distinct from Recommendation 3.1.2 favoring buffered crystalloids over
0.9% saline for other patient groups at risk of AKI/AKD.

Practice Point 4.9.2.2: In the absence of volume overload, it is appropriate to hold
diuretics when administering isotonic crystalloid to prevent CA-AKI in adults at high
risk.
Several trials have suggested that prophylactic diuretic administration increases the
incidence of CA-AKI, potentially by causing hypovolemia,666-668 although this has been an
inconsistent finding.669 Furthermore, several trials have found that matching hydration to
forced diuresis using furosemide can reduce the incidence of CA-AKI, when delivered using
of an integrated device matching saline fluid replacement to urine flow rate.625, 627
In a recent Cochrane review, prophylactic furosemide did not decrease the incidence
of CA-AKI in an analysis of 8 trials involving 1886 participants (RR: 0.87; 95% CI: 0.57–
1.34, P=0.54). Findings were similar in a subanalysis of 5 of these trials (n=1604
participants) that reported RRT incidence post-contrast (RR: 0.76; 95% CI: 0.35–1.65;
P=0.48).427 This review also included trials that compared the use of furosemide together
with an integrated device to match saline administration to urine flow rate versus saline
alone. A total of 1302 participants were included in 2 trials of people receiving coronary
232

angiography, and 3 trials of transcatheter aortic valve replacement. There was no difference
in CA-AKI between furosemide-forced diuresis with device-guided urine output replacement
versus saline hydration (RR: 0.83; 95% CI: 0.48–1.44; P=0.51). Further, among the subgroup
of 8 trials (n=930 participants) comparing furosemide without an integrated urine flow rate
monitoring and fluid replacement device to saline hydration, the former did not significantly
change the incidence of CA-AKI (RR: 0.83; 95% CI: 0.53–1.28; P=0.39).427 However, it

4.10 Temporary withdrawal of medications to prevent AKI during major surgery
Recommendation 4.10.1: We suggest not routinely withholding renin-angiotensin

Key information
