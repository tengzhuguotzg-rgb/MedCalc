# Chapter 1: AKI and AKD Definition, Identification, and Classification

AND CLASSIFICATION
The definition of acute kidney impairment has evolved from a fragmented landscape
of disparate terms into a unified framework of Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) and Acute Kidney
Disease (AKD). While the field has relied on serum creatinine (SCr) and urine output since
the standardization efforts of 2009, the shift toward consistent Kidney Disease: Improving
Global Outcomes (KDIGO)-based staging has been instrumental in improving global
T
epidemiological tracking and clinical risk stratification. However, as SCr often serves as a
trailing marker of kinetic change, the current frontier of nephrology emphasizes integrating
novel biomarkers and updated evidence to identify kidney stress earlier. This chapter
explores the transition from these traditional fuFnctional markers to a modern, precision-based
approach to diagnosis and classification.

presence of one or more abnormalities of kidney function or structure known or
presumed to have occurred within 7 days, in the appropriate clinical context (Table 1).

The KDIGO 2012 Clinical Practice Guideline for Acute Kidney Injury provided a
definition for AKI based on changes in SCr and urine output that are associated with the risks
of death and receipt of renal replacement therapy (RRT) in hospitalized patients.1 SCr and
urine output are markers of kidney function, specifically glomerular filtration rate (GFR).
Both measures are widely used in routine clinical practice and can be obtained at relatively
low cost. However, neither measure is entirely sensitive or specific to GFR and can lead to
misclassification of AKI (Table 2).

The development and validation of several novel biomarkers over the last decade has
enabled earlier detection of kidney cellular stress, damage, or inflammation compared with
the use of functional markers alone, thus enabling earlier intervention and potentially
improving outcomes.2 Further, alternative functional biomarkers (i.e., serum cystatin C) are
now available that overcome some of the limitations of SCr. For instance, cystatin C is not
confounded by muscle mass. However, these markers have their own limitations, as well
(Recommendation 1.1.1).
The updated definition adds new criteria for the purpose of AKI detection based on
abnormalities of kidney structure, which reflect cellular stress, damage, or inflammation that
may occur in the presence or absence of reduced kidney function. These new structural
criteria for AKI are intended to encompass a number of urine or serum biomarkers that have
received regulatory qualification for use in specific clinical settings based in part on their
ability to predict the development of AKI before functional criteria are met (Table 3 and 4).
36

These biomarkers may become elevated before an increase in SCr or reduction in
urine output occurs; a state that has previously been termed “subclinical AKI”. Examples of
these biomarkers include urinary tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases-2 (TIMP-2), insulin-
like growth factor binding protein 7 (IGFBP7), neutrophil gelatinase-associated lipoprotein
(NGAL), kidney injury molecule‑1 (KIM-1), and liver-type fatty acid-binding protein (L-
FABP).
A common method of categorizing kidney biomarkers is to stratify them based on
their characteristics. Thus, some biomarkers primarily reflect glomerular filtration (i.e. serum
T
cystatin C) whereas others represent tubular cell stress (i.e., IGFBP7 and TIMP-2) or tubular
cell damage [i.e. NGAL, KIM-1, NAG, L-FABP]. Prolonged and increased expression of
TIMP-2 and IGFBP7 may be synonymous with cell damage. Other kidney biomarkers
indicate intrarenal inflammation (i.e. IL-18, IL-9, CCL14).
F
All biomarkers have specific characteristics, are released in response to particular
triggers and have unique kinetic profiles, including half-life and time to peak value (Table 4).
Many are elevated in AKI but also confounded by different medical conditions or
A
interventions. Importantly, several studies in the literature used different cut-offs to
distinguish between “biomarker-positive” versus “biomarker-negative” conditions, which
explains some of the differences in performance but also makes direct comparisons between
studies challenging.
R
It is important to keep these aspects in mind when measuring and interpreting
biomarker values in specific patient cohorts. When deciding whether to integrate a biomarker
into routine monitoring or to include a biomarker in the diagnostic work-up of a specific
clinDical setting, it is essential to select one that is physiologically and biologically relevant to
the question of interest. Further, timing of measurement needs to be guided by the kinetic
profile of the biomarker.
The availability and higher costs of new biomarker tests limit their use in many
settings but it is possible that earlier detection of AKI may ultimately offset these costs due to
lower resource utilization and healthcare spending.

Recommendation 1.1.1: We suggest using serum cystatin C to identify AKI in children
and adults in clinical situations when serum creatinine (SCr) is less accurate (2B).
This recommendation puts a high value on the greater sensitivity of cystatin C for detecting
AKI in specific patient populations where changes in SCr can be insensitive. The
recommendation places a lower value on resource use and additional costs. The feasibility of
implementing this recommendation is highly dependent on the availability and turnaround
time for cystatin C measurement.
T
Key information
Balance of benefits and harms
Previous AKI criteria using SCr and urine output measurements were established
based on risks of kidney failure and mortality.1, 12 Cystatin C is an alternative endogenous
F
filtration marker that provides a more accurate GFR estimate than SCr in some specific
populations. In a study of 38 mechanically ventilated adults in the intensive care unit (ICU),
longer ICU stay was associated with greater differences between GFR estimated by serum
cystatin C and SCr, suggesting thatA SCr can systematically overestimate GFR after prolonged
critical illness.13 A recent modeling study found that serum cystatin C can achieve steady-
state concentration and meet AKI diagnostic thresholds significantly faster than SCr, with an
advantage of 6–48 hours, depending on chronic kidney disease (CKD) severity. Serum
cystatin C had greater sensitivity in detecting GFR reductions, with the ability to detect AKI
R
within 12–24 hours after an insult, compared with 12–72 hours for SCr.14
Several meta-analyses have evaluated the diagnostic performance of cystatin C for
early AKI identification. A meta-analysis by Lan et al. included 4 diagnostic accuracy
D
studies that evaluated the performance of serum cystatin C, most often measured within 6–12
hours of major surgery in adults, using a reference standard of later development of AKI
defined by Risk, Injury, Failure, Loss of kidney function, and End-stage kidney disease
(RIFLE), Acute Kidney Injury Network (AKIN), or the KDIGO 2012 AKI criteria
(Supplementary Tables S5).15 Serum cystatin C exhibited relatively high discrimination with
an area under the receiver-operating characteristics curve (AUROC) of 0.82 (95% confidence
interval [CI]: 0.78–0.85). In an accompanying network meta-analysis, serum cystatin C
exhibited the best discrimination and had the greatest specificity for early AKI detection
among the 7 biomarkers included in the analysis.15 Evidence review of 16 pediatric studies
found that serum cystatin C had moderate accuracy to predict incident AKI (AUC 0.80) and
moderate or severe AKI (AUC 0.83) in children (Supplementary Table S6).16
Certainty of evidence
In adults, there is moderate certainty of evidence that serum cystatin C has
“moderate” accuracy as an early predictor of post-major surgery AKI, defined as an AUROC
curve of between 0.75 and 0.85. The certainty of evidence was downgraded for some
methodological limitations of the studies.
45

In children, there is moderate certainty of evidence that serum cystatin C has
“moderate” accuracy as an early predictor of incident AKI and Stage 2–3 AKI, defined as an
AUROC of between 0.75 and 0.85. The certainty of evidence was downgraded for some
methodological limitations of the studies, in addition to imprecision of the accuracy of Stage
2–3 AKI.
Values and preferences
This recommendation places a high value on the need for accurate and timely
T
detection of a decline in GFR. The Work Group judged that healthcare professionals and
most people at risk for AKI would prefer to use a more accurate test enabling earlier
identification of AKI in situations where changes in SCr are insensitive or nonspecific.
However, these benefits need to be balanced against the additional consequences and costs
F
associated with cystatin C testing. The Work Group therefore prioritized reserving use of
cystatin C for AKI detection to the clinical situations provided in Table 5 where more
accurate measurement may alter clinical decisions.
A
Resource use and costs
Costs incurred for the higher frequency of cystatin C testing include one-time costs
associated with initiation of the assay within a laboratory including building the information
technology infrastructure and method verification studies; and continuous costs associated
R
with consumables and maintaining the assay. If cystatin C is performed in an outside
laboratory, other costs may ensue as with any laboratory test. Additional costs may also
result from increased referrals to nephrologists to assist in interpretation of potentially
discordant results between SCr and cystatin C results. Ideally, these costs would decrease
D
over time with increased utilization and familiarity with the properties of the test.
Considerations for implementation
Implementation of this recommendation requires cystatin C testing be available in
acute care centers with results available in a timely manner. Guidance to clinical laboratories
on implementation and laboratory standards for cystatin C testing is provided in the KDIGO
2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for CKD (Practice Points 1.2.3.1 and 1.2.3.2).17 Due to the
increased costs of cystatin C testing, clear indications for testing with cystatin C in addition
to SCr should be developed within institutions and SCr should remain the primary laboratory
tests for AKI identification in the majority of patients. Education for healthcare professionals,
caregivers, and people with AKI for optimal use and interpretation of cystatin C results is
required.
Rationale
Changes in SCr may be insensitive for detection of AKI in acute and chronic illnesses
that affect liver function and muscle mass (Table 2). In these scenarios, serum cystatin C may
offer greater accuracy than SCr for early detection of AKI and opportunities for intervention.
46

Potential harms include false positive test results, overdiagnosis, and the increased health
care costs associated with the test itself as well as follow-up testing. In addition, discordant
results between SCr and cystatin C may lead to increased nephrology consultations.
Obtaining cystatin C results may take longer than SCr result depending on the local
laboratory infrastructure and processes, which may lead to delays in recognition and offset
the benefits of its use if results are not reported in a timely manner. Table 5 outlines specific
situations where cystatin C measurement may be preferable to SCr to detect reduced GFR.

Practice Point 1.1.2: A baseline SCr value should be ascertained for all patients with
suspected or confirmed AKI.
The KDIGO SCr criteria for AKI are based on an increase from a baseline value
D
known or presumed to have occurred over a short time frame. Identification of an accurate
baseline value is therefore required to establish whether a specific SCr value represents a
change and whether the change is sufficient to meet AKI criteria. This is also needed for
staging the severity of AKI based on the magnitude of SCr increase and for ascertaining the
extent of recovery of GFR following AKI. A baseline SCr should be representative of a
patient’s GFR before the onset of AKI. In clinical practice, variation in the extent of SCr
testing for a patient will influence the selection of a representative SCr value as the baseline
value. Access to previous laboratory results obtained prior to an acute illness is helpful in
establishing an appropriate baseline. In their absence, several approaches have been
recommended in the literature including use of a SCr representative of normal GFR for a
patient’s age and sex, or the use of a subsequent lower SCr value when SCr values are
declining in the absence of RRT.
47

There are several different approaches described for ascertaining baseline SCr values
(Supplementary Tables S7 and S8). Therefore, we advise using a mean, median, or most
D
recent SCr measurement between 8–365 days prior to admission or acute event as the
preferred method to establish baseline kidney function representing a stable clinical state.
Differences in methods for establishing baseline SCr can affect whether patients meet AKI
criteria leading to both over- and underdiagnosis. Underdiagnosis of AKI can lead to delayed
recognition thereby delaying investigations and treatment. Overdiagnosis of AKI in people
with CKD may expose patients to unwarranted tests and interventions. In addition, use of
varying methods in epidemiological studies impacts the comparability of findings, limiting
interpretation of consistency of findings across different geographic regions and clinical
settings.
Access to outpatient SCr values may not be available in some settings. Wherever
possible, systems should ideally be integrated or if this is not possible, primary care and
outpatient values should be accessible to clinicians in secondary care settings, and vice versa.
Education of healthcare professionals is required to ensure optimum implementation of this
recommendation.
48

In the absence of an available outpatient SCr within the last year, we advise using SCr
on admission as the next option unless AKI is suspected on admission (e.g., community-
acquired AKI). This provides a readily available value that is more appropriate for patients
admitted for elective procedures (e.g., surgery, imaging procedures) but may be affected by
conditions which have precipitated nonelective admission for conditions such as volume
depletion or sepsis. An alternative to this approach is the use of the minimum value during
admission including post-AKI values, allowing for retrospective AKI diagnosis. This
approach is less desirable due to the influence of fluctuations or interventions that might
lower SCr, such as fluid administration or withdrawal of medications that lower GFR and
T
would not be representative of the true baseline in patients who have not fully recovered
kidney function.
In the absence of any available measurements, we advise back calculating, assuming
F
an estimated GFR (eGFR) of 75 ml/min per 1.73 m2 for adults, based on a validated eGFR
estimating equation is acceptable.18 Use of an equation with greater accuracy at higher eGFR,
such as the Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration Equation (CKD EPI)
equation, is preferable to equationsA that have been previously recommended such as the
Modification of Diet in Renal Disease (MDRD) equation. However, assuming a baseline
GFR of 75 ml/min per 1.73 m2 for adults does not account for individual patient
characteristics and the presence of underlying CKD. Studies have shown that this method
leads to both over- and underestimation of AKI.19-24 Approaches specific to calculating a
R
baseline SCr in children are discussed in Practice Point 1.6.2.
For the purpose of epidemiological research, use the median of outpatient values 7–
365 days prior to admission or an acute event is advised. It is important to ensure that there is
D
a uniform approach to defining AKI for epidemiological studies, allowing for valid
comparisons between studies and for analyses of trends over time.

1.5. Recurrent AKI
Practice Point 1.5.1: Recurrent AKI is defined as a new episode of AKI occurring after
partial or complete resolution from a previous episode of AKI.
DRecurrent AKI is associated with adverse outcomes.46, 50, 55 However, there is
considerable variation in how recurrent AKI has been defined previously. Liu et al. defined
recurrent AKI in adults as being rehospitalized with a SCr level increase by ≥0.3 mg/dl
within 48 hours of readmission or ≥50% above a “baseline” SCr level.55 Walker et al.
defined recurrent AKI as meeting the biochemical definition of AKI within the observation
period of 6 years50 and Altawalbeh et al., within 5 years.46
In the Assessment of Worldwide Acute Kidney injury Epidemiology in Neonates
(AWAKEN) cohort study of 2162 critically ill neonates, recurrent AKI was defined as a new
AKI episode occurring only after SCr had returned to the prior baseline.36 Among neonates
with AKI, 22% experienced recurrent AKI. These infants had significantly longer hospital
stays than those with a single episode of AKI (median 60 vs. 18 days). Recurrent AKI was
also independently associated with reduced likelihood of discharge (adjusted HR: 0.7; 95%
CI: 0.6–0.9). Similarly, in a retrospective cohort of preterm neonates ≤28 weeks gestation,
Adegboyega et al. found that 24% developed recurrent AKI.47 Early-onset and severe initial
AKI were associated with increased risk for recurrent AKI, which in turn was linked with a
58

significantly longer hospital stay (+36 days) and increased mortality (adjusted OR: 4.55; 95%
CI: 1.12–18.51) compared to a single episode of AKI.
The Work Group deemed that the definition of recurrent AKI be consistent with the
proposed resolution definition (Table 10), so that each episode of AKI is identified as a
separate event, based on a new baseline SCr or cystatin C where warranted, without imposing
a timeframe between a first and subsequent AKI episode. A standardized definition will
allow for comparison of predictors, incidence, and prognosis across studies, and could aid in
distinguishing patients with new episodes warranting assessment from those with sequelae of
T
AKD or CKD.

Use of neonatal-specific thresholds for AKI staging enables more appropriate risk
stratification for morbidity and mortality that can inform clinical management and follow-up.
Large absolute changes in SCr are uncommon in neonates due to low muscle mass and
physiologically low baseline GFR. The SCr criteria for Stage 3 AKI are reduced to 2.5 mg/dl
in neonates, which represents a GFR of <10 ml/min per 1.73 m2. However, it is important to
recognize that even this reduced threshold may still be too high, particularly in preterm
neonates with inherently lower baseline SCr values due to smaller muscle mass. In these
populations, a SCr level of 2.5 mg/dl may reflect profound abnormal kidney function. Further
study is warranted to refine this cutoff in preterm infants and better align AKI staging with
meaningful clinical outcomes in this vulnerable group. In resource-rich settings, the inclusion
of additional damage biomarkers such as NGAL and filtration markers such as serum
cystatin C may enhance AKI severity staging.

The use of neonatal-specific criteria introduces additional complexity and may be
influenced by delayed creatinine kinetics or transient physiologic oliguria in neonates. There
is a small risk of overestimating the severity of AKI using these criteria, particularly in
preterm infants or those with non-kidney comorbidities that influence fluid status and
creatinine handling. This may lead to a greater utilization of healthcare resources, including
biomarker testing and nephrology consultations. These extra demands on resources are
expected to be balanced by earlier recognition and timely treatment due to the high risk of
morbidity and mortality associated with AKI severity in neonates. The potential for timely
