The risk of AKI is altered by the presence of factors that increase susceptibility to
AKI in combination with exposures that cause AKI. There are several features that determine
a person’s susceptibility to AKI including demographic characteristics, genetics, social and
T
environmental determinants, as well as comorbidities (acute or chronic conditions).
Preexisting CKD and previous episodes of AKI represent major susceptibilities. Common
exposures include conditions that alter fluid balance or hemodynamic status, infections, and
nephrotoxic medications, which are more likely to lead to AKI in susceptible individuals.

generally recognized that a person’s degree of susceptibility and intensity of exposures
together increase their risk of developing AKI (Figure 4).

Recognition of a person’s risk factors for developing AKI and associated adverse
outcomes can be useful to target prevention and management strategies to people at risk.
However, the risk of AKI changes in an individual person over time. Although it can be
estimated at a given moment in time (e.g., at a preoperative clinic assessment or upon ICU
admission), it is important to recognize that a person’s risk is dynamic over the course of
illness. Further, some exposures may be modifiable, and addressing these can reduce the risk
of AKI. Susceptibilities and exposures for AKI can differ between community and hospital
settings (Table 14). While there has been increasing knowledge of the epidemiology and
outcomes of community-acquired AKI detected in hospital as well as community-acquired

AKI detected in primary care, most current knowledge has been obtained from research on
people in hospitals. Importantly, susceptibilities and exposures may interact in non-linear
ways, such that the same exposure can confer markedly different AKI risk depending on
baseline kidney function, frailty, comorbidity burden, and acute illness severity.

2.1. Risk prediction models
Recommendation 2.1.1: We suggest using externally validated clinical risk models to
identify children and adults at increased risk of AKI and guide the use of kidney-
protective strategies (2C).
This recommendation emphasizes the use of externally validated clinical risk models for
early identification of children and adults at increased risk of AKI. Such models integrate
multiple patient-level and contextual risk factors and have demonstrated significant
discrimination and calibration for predicting AKI, supporting more consistent and objective
risk stratification. Applying these tools in susceptible populations may enable more
appropriate and efficient care through timely implementation of kidney-protective strategies
to people at higher risk. This approach can also avoid unnecessary testing and intervention
in people who are unlikely to benefit. Overall, this approach supports proactive, evidence-
informed personalized care aimed at reducing AKI incidence and associated morbidity.

Key information
Balance of benefits and harms
The primary benefit of using validated risk models is improved identification of
children and adults at increased risk for AKI, enabling earlier and more targeted
implementation of kidney-protective strategies in people at high risk, which may reduce AKI
incidence, severity, and downstream complications. These models can standardize risk
assessment, reduce reliance on subjective clinical judgment, aTnd support more efficient use
of preventive interventions in susceptible populations. Potential harms include
overestimation of risk in some individuals, which could lead to unnecessary monitoring,
interventions, or avoidance of beneficial therapies for underlying conditions. There is also a
risk that poor integration of models into clinical sFystems can contribute to workflow burden.
Embedding prediction models as electronic health record (EHR) alerts without linked action
pathways may increase alert fatigue and reduce the effectiveness of decision support
interventions. Overall, the anticipated benefits of more accurate risk stratification and
A
targeted prevention are judged to outweigh the generally low and manageable potential
harms when validated models are applied thoughtfully.

validated AKI prediRction models used in various populations at risk for AKI found
substantial heterogeneity in model performance, though models generally demonstrated good
discrimination upon external validation for prediction of AKI (overall pooled area under
curve [AUC] 0.78, range 0.75–0.84 across hospitalization, ICU, post-contrast, and
postoperative settings (Supplementary Table S16).100 The cumulative evidence suggests most
D

AKI complications in hospitalized adults, supporting their use in care pathways to identify
high-risk surgical and critically ill populations. These models enable early identification of
people at elevated AKI risk but may also facilitate the targeted application of kidney-
protective strategies. However, there is a high degree of inconsistency across studies
regarding clinical settings, patient populations and predictive performance. Furthermore,
models used different criteria for AKI. The predictive performance of models for the revised
KDIGO AKI criteria is unknown.

Values and preferences
Identification of people at high risk of developing any form of AKI or progression to
severe AKI or needing RRT is viewed positively by well-informed patients, in particular if
the predictive models are based on routinely available data, do not require any additional
investigations, are sufficiently accurate, and offer the prospect of potential preventive
interventions to avoid AKI and subsequent adverse outcomes in vulnerable people.
Resource use and costs
Utilizing and applying most validated AKI risk models generally does not require
significant additional direct costs for the patient or institution, as they primarily rely on
healthcare professionals executing models using existing clinical data entered into an integer
scoring system or computer-based calculator. The primary expense with systematic
implementation of predictive models is the upfront and ongoing cost of integrating and
updating (when necessary) the risk calculation within the existing information technology
infrastructure of a hospital, such as an EHR. Further costs should be incurred to ensure the
model continues to accurately calculate risk while other costs may arise from additional
70

testing or treatments being applied to high-risk people. These potential harms may also be
increased by the degree a model falsely classifies people as being at high risk for AKI, which
could result in more unnecessary interventions and increased costs. However, overall, the
primary economic argument for integrating AKI risk models into care is that they may lead
to more targeted prevention efforts for AKI and related complications in people who are
more likely to benefit, reducing the need for scarce or expensive downstream resources for
care (e.g., ICU admission, RRT).110

Considerations for implementation
Successful implementation of AKI risk models requires initial investment and
attention to specific clinical, technical, and institutional factors that impact model
performance, uptake, and outcomes. Predictive riFsk models are only valuable if they are
based on accurate and reliably collected predictor variables and delivered in a manner that
supports clinical judgement. Implementation will achieve greater value when risk
stratification is linked to specific, evidence-based interventions that can improve patient care
A
and outcomes.
Rationale
Risk prediction models combine multiple patient-, procedure-, and/or care-related
factors into a structuRred output on the probability of a future event for an individual person,
which facilitates risk stratification and can guide the timely deployment of kidney-protective
strategies in susceptible populations. Different risk models have been developed to predict
AKI within specified time horizons and using variables ascertained as specific time points;
for instance; for adults undergoing cardiac surgery (e.g., Mehta score; Supplementary Table
D
S17) or major non-cardiac surgery (e.g., SPARK score; Supplementary Table S18), for
people in the hospital, for people in the ICU,100, 111, 112 and people with sepsis.113, 114 In
addition, risk prediction models have been developed and validated specifically for pediatric
populations across different clinical settings, including hospitalized children,115, 116 children
in the pediatric intensive care unit (PICU),117, 118 pediatric cardiac surgery patients,119 and
children with septic shock120 (Recommendation 2.6.1; Supplementary Table S19). Model
performance can vary depending on the specific patient population and features of the model.
Significant heterogeneity in discrimination has been identified between externally validated
models for AKI,100 necessitating careful selection of models shown to have adequate
performance and clinical utility in the population and clinical setting they will be used.
Most AKI risk models assess risk at a single time point, such as pre-surgery or upon
ICU admission. Some newer models are dynamic, updating scores with data collected
throughout the hospital stay. Examples include models adding intra- and postoperative data
to improve the ability to predict postoperative AKI.121, 122 Similarly, other studies have
demonstrated that risk assessment improves with adding variables from day 1 or day 2 of a
71

non-postoperative ICU stay.111, 123 Thus, dynamic prediction of risk of AKI over time may
provide insights into which patients warrant greater focus on preventive interventions over
the course of an illness trajectory.

Values and preferences
The initiation of RRT represents an escalation in medical care requirements,
accompanied by increased utilization of healthcare resources, increased workload for
healthcare professionals, prolonged hospitalization, and elevated healthcare expenditures.

want to avoid RRT, and if required, may benefit from being made aware and prepared to

preventive interventions, patient-centered counselling, earlier nephrology consultation, and
timely preparation of RRT, including potential transfer to appropriate clinical settings such as
ICU or a site with dialysis services.

Use of validated risk models generally requires modest resources, as most models
rely on routinely collected clinical and laboratory data already available in electronic health
records. Implementation costs may include integration into clinical workflows or electronic

additional interventions and unnecessary transferF to an ICU or dialysis center. Earlier and
better-informed shared decision-making may also reduce potentially avoidable or unwanted

efficiency and patient-centered outcomes.
Considerations for implementation
Implementation of this recommendation requires selection of validated risk models
for RRT that are appRropriate for the local patient population and clinical setting. This is
particularly important in rural and low-resource settings, as clinical practice patterns, access,
and resources for RRT differ across jurisdictions necessitating steps to determine whether a
model developed to predict RRT elsewhere (often a high-resource setting) provides accurate
information in another setting.129, 132-138 Integration of models into existing clinical
D
workflows and EHRs is important to ensure timely access to risk estimates without adding
undue burden to healthcare professionals. Healthcare professionals should receive training on
model interpretation, limitations, and how to communicate risk clearly and sensitively to
support shared decision-making. Risk models should be used as adjuncts to, rather than
replacements for, clinical judgment and individualized patient assessment. Ongoing
evaluation of model performance, equity, and patient understanding is important to ensure
appropriate and effective use in practice.
Rationale

2.2. Electronic AKI alerts
Recommendation 2.2.1: We recommend against delivering interruptive, creatinine-
based electronic alerts for AKI in isolation without systematic strategies for response in
routine clinical care of adults (1B).
This recommendation applies to interruptive electronic alerts triggered by changes in SCr
and implemented without an accompanying, structured response pathway. Electronic alert
systems require significant effort and information technology infrastructure to develop and
maintain. Evidence indicates that alerts may increase AKI recognition and have modest
effects on clinical processes of care but alone do not consistently improve patient outcomes
and may contribute to alert fatigue. If electronic AKI alerts are used, they should be
78

embedded within a comprehensive care strategy that includes clear clinical guidance,
accountability for response, and integration with multidisciplinary workflows aimed at timely
evaluation and management of AKI.
Key information
Balance of benefits and harms
Electronic alerts to identify people with AKI can increase awareness of changes in
kidney function and prompt earlier recognition of AKI. HoweTver, in clinical trials where
SCr-based AKI alerts have been evaluated without standardized response protocols, clinical
decision support, or accountability structures, they have not been shown to improve patient-
centered outcomes such as mortality, need for RRT, or recovery of kidney function.
F

versus no alerts in a total of 29,979 adults at risk of AKI showed no significant differences in
mortality (RR: 0.96; 95% CI: 0.91–1.02), progression to Stage 3 AKI (RR: 1.04; 95% CI:
A
0.79–1.36), need for RRT (RR: 1.02; 95% CI: 0.92–1.13), or development of MAKE
(adjusted OR: 0.83; 95% CI: 0.67–1.03; Supplementary Table S20). There were also no
differences in duration of AKI or chances of recovery of kidney function.
Potential harRms of AKI include alert fatigue, workflow disruption, inappropriate or
delayed clinical decision-making, overtesting, and therapeutic inertia due to overreliance on
nonspecific SCr changes. Alerts based solely on SCr may also misclassify AKI, particularly

reduce GFR, leading to unnecessary interventions or consultations. Interruptive alerts can

impose cognitive burden on healthcare professionals and may divert attention from higher-

widespread implementation of isolated alerts consumes informatics and clinical resources
without clear evidence of benefit and may erode healthcare professionals trust in electronic
decision support tools.
Certainty of evidence
There is moderate certainty of evidence that, in adults, delivery of SCr-based
electronic alerts for AKI identification alone, without systematic care strategies, do not

kidney function and will provide misleading estimates of GFR in the setting of rapidly
changing SCr concentration. Kinetic estimated glomerular filtration rate (KeGFR)
incorporates the rate of change in SCr, providing a more physiologically appropriate

nephrotoxic exposure, or escalation of care depend on an estimate of GFR, KeGFR may offer

Key information
Balance of benefits and harms
The kinetic KeGFR is designed to estimate kidney function under non-steady-state
D
conditions by evaluating serial SCr measurements. Unlike traditional eGFR equations, which
assume steady-state kinetics, KeGFR accounts for rapid fluctuations in SCr that occur in
evolving AKI. Several equations, most notably the Chen formula, have been developed to
interpret SCr kinetics in dynamic settings.182

during nonsteady-state conditions, when SCr is changing and conventional eGFR equations
(that assume steady-state SCr) may be inaccurate. More accurate assessment of real-time

avoidance of nephrotoxins, or use of contrast, potentially reducing preventable harm. KeGFR
may also enhance healthcare professional understanding of the trajectory and severity of
AKI, which can inform care planning.

Values and preferences
Well-informed patients, caregivers, and healthcare professionals generally place a
high value on early recognition of risk, particularly when progression to severe AKI may
lead to significant morbidity, need for RRT, or prolonged hospitalization. The use of
validated biomarkers of kidney stress or damage in combination with clinical assessment
aligns with this value by offering earlier and more objective information than traditional
measures alone, supporting timely and proactive kidney-protective care in high-risk acute
care settings.
Preferences may vary regarding the use of additional testing, especially if results will
not clearly change management or if there is uncertainty about interpretation. Some patients
and caregivers may prioritize minimizing tests, costs, or anxiety associated with abnormal
results. Healthcare professionals may also differ in their comfort and familiarity with
99

biomarker-guided strategies. These variations in values and preferences support a Level 2
statement, emphasizing selective use according to validated indications.
Resource use and cost
Use of additional biomarkers entails additional direct costs related to setting up the
laboratory assays as an in-house test to provide timely results, as well as per test costs. These
costs vary by biomarker, testing frequency, and local laboratory infrastructure, and are higher
than those of conventional kidney function tests. Implementation may also require
investment in healthcare professional education, protocol development, and integration of
T
results into clinical workflows or EHRs to ensure appropriate use of results. In high-resource
settings, where laboratory infrastructure, regulatory approval, and supply chains are
established, biomarker testing can be integrated into routine workflows with predictable per-
test costs and rapid turnaround times. In low-resource settings, limited access to biomarker
F
assays, higher relative costs, lack of analyzer platforms, or longer turnaround times may
substantially increase resource burden or make testing not feasible. In such contexts, routine
biomarker use could divert resources from other essential aspects of care without clear added
value. Selective or restricted use, wAhen available, may be more appropriate, and reliance on
clinical assessment and conventional tests may remain the primary strategy.
Biomarker-guided risk stratification may lead to more efficient use of healthcare
resources by enabling targeted kidney-protective strategies in people most likely to benefit,
R
while avoiding unnecessary interventions in those at lower-risk. Earlier identification of
people at risk of severe AKI has the potential to reduce downstream costs associated with
AKI complications; however, findings from cost-effectiveness analyses have been highly
uncertain.222 The BigpAK trial reported a reduction in overall cost of care following
D
implementation of [TIMP-2]*[IGFBP7] measurement through improved AKI care processes
and shorter lengths of stay.5 Thus, while upfront testing costs are higher, emerging evidence
suggests that selective use according to validated and approved indications may result in a

major surgery).
Cost-effectiveness is likely to depend on baseline AKI risk/incidence in the tested
population and whether biomarker results are linked to an actionable intervention and thus
may vary substantially across clinical settings and health systems.
Considerations for implementation
Accessibility and availability of biomarker tests are important considerations for
implementation, particularly across settings with varying resource levels.223 In high-resource
settings, these tests may be readily available with rapid turnaround times and electronic
integration, facilitating timely risk stratification and protocolized kidney-protective
responses. In contrast, limited laboratory infrastructure, higher costs, and lack of regulatory
100

approval restricts availability in low-resource settings, making routine or widespread use

Implementation strategies should therefore be tailored to local capacity; prioritizing
biomarker use in clinical scenarios where the added prognostic value is greatest and where
results can realistically influence management. Clear protocols should define appropriate
indications, timing of testing, and thresholds for action to ensure biomarkers are used
consistently and inform management. Healthcare professionals and healthcare teams should
receive training on the appropriate interpretation of biomarker results and their limitations,
T
emphasizing biomarkers as adjuncts to, rather than replacements for, clinical assessment.

Practice Point 2.5.4: Urine chemistries, including fractional excretion of sodium (FeNa)
and fractional excretion of urea (FeUrea) should be reserved for use in differentiating

factors, including diuretic exposure, CKD, and recent fluid or vasoactive therapy are often

biomarkers of tubular stress or damage provide more insight into AKI pathophysiology than
urine chemistries alone.

Practice Point 2.5.5: In adults with cirrhosis-associated AKI, FeNa can be an adjunctive tool to
distinguish hepatorenal syndrome from ATI when interpreted in the appropriate clinical
context.
Cirrhosis-associated AKI encompasses a heterogeneous group of pathophysiologic
processes, including hepatorenal syndrome, ATI, and mixed or overlapping phenotypes.
Differentiating hepatorenal syndrome from ATI has important therapeutic and prognostic
implications, particularly with respect to the use of vasoconstrictor therapy, albumin
administration, and decisions regarding RRT and liver transplant evaluation.252
T
FeNa may retain discriminatory value in cirrhosis, owing to the distinct renal sodium-
handling physiology characteristic of hepatorenal syndrome. Hepatorenal syndrome is
marked by intense renal vasoconstriction with preserved tubular integrity, resulting in avid
F
sodium retention and typically very low FeNa values. In comparison, ATI in cirrhosis is
associated with structural tubular damage, impaired sodium reabsorption, and relatively
higher FeNa values.
A
Several observational studies have demonstrated that very low FeNa values (often
<0.1%–0.2%) are more consistent with hepatorenal syndrome, whereas higher values
increase the likelihood of tubular injury,246, 253 particularly when supported by additional
clinical and laboratory features (e.g., urinary sediment, lack of response to volume
R
expansion, exposure to nephrotoxins, or ischemic insults). However, important limitations
should be recognized. FeNa values may be affected by diuretic exposure, recent volume
administration, sepsis or systemic inflammation. Further, mixed hepatorenal syndrome–ATI
phenotypes are common in advanced cirrhosis.252, 254, 255 Still, in studies comparing FeNa
with DFeUrea in people with liver disease, FeNa consistently outperformed FeUrea for
distinguishing hepatorenal syndrome from other forms of AKI.256, 257
Accordingly, FeNa should not be used in isolation to establish the diagnosis of
hepatorenal syndrome or ATI. Rather, it should be interpreted alongside clinical criteria for
hepatorenal syndrome, urine sediment findings, and the overall clinical trajectory. The Work
Group agreed with consensus statements from the International Club of Ascites (ICA) and
the ADQI emphasizing that while urinary indices such as FeNa may provide supportive data,
diagnosis of hepatorenal syndrome should not rely solely on these values due to their variable
sensitivity and limited specificity in complex clinical settings.252, 258

Practice Point 2.5.7: Kidney biopsy should be considered for children and adults with
unexplained AKI when clinical evaluation does not clearly differentiate among potential
causes or the findings are expected to influence management decisions.
Kidney biopsy may be considered in people with unexplained AKI when clinical
evaluation does not clearly differentiate among potential etiologies or when histopathological
diagnosis is expected to influence clinical decision-making. While many cases of AKI can be
managed based on clinical and laboratory data alone, a subset of people present with
diagnostic uncertainty, atypical presentations, or nonresolving injury, where biopsy may
110

provide crucial insights that guide management. Biopsy findings can clarify ambiguous
presentations, identify treatable diseases, prevent therapeutic misdirection, and enable
personalized care.
Studies have consistently shown that histologic diagnoses obtained from kidney
biopsies for AKI can diverge from clinical impressions. In a large study conducted in the
1990s in the U.S. of 259 older adults who received a native kidney biopsy for AKI, biopsy
yielded a definitive diagnosis in over 90% of cases, kidney biopsy diagnosis differed from
the prebiopsy clinical diagnosis in 33% of cases, and a diagnosis offering the potential for
T
improved outcomes with treatment was deemed to have been made in 73% of the cases.263
Kidney biopsy findings also provide prognostic information. In a Korean multicenter cohort,
biopsy-confirmed ATN and AIN had distinct outcomes, with AIN conferring a higher risk of
progression to kidney failure.264
F
Regional biopsy series further highlight the diagnostic value of histopathologic
assessment. In a case series of patients biopsied for AKI from a tertiary care center in South
India, AIN was the most commonlyA diagnosed lesion.265 A systematic review by Wen et al.
confirmed significant diversity in the histopathologic manifestations of ATI and noted
limited correlation with clinical phenotypes.266 In the setting of COVID-19, biopsies have
revealed a wide spectrum of kidney pathologies, including collapsing glomerulopathy, ATN,
and minimal change disease, which were not clinically apparent.267, 268 This diagnostic
R
variability highlights the limitations of relying solely on noninvasive assessments in complex
or diagnostically uncertain clinical scenarios.
The Kidney Precision Medicine Project (KPMP) reinforces the diagnostic value of
biopsDy, to integrate kidney tissue analysis with molecular and clinical data, demonstrating
significant pathologic and transcriptomic heterogeneity in AKI, even among clinically
similar patients.269 These findings support broader use of kidney biopsy, helping to uncover
disease sub-endotypes and potential therapeutic targets.270 Biopsy is particularly critical
when etiologic certainty is important for subsequent management decisions, such as with
immune checkpoint inhibitor associated AKI. A case series by Rashidi et al. illustrated
presentations where people with presumed AIN based on clinical grounds had ATI on
biopsy, which allowed continuation of oncologic immunotherapy and avoided inappropriate
change of immunosuppression.271
Concerns around procedural risks of kidney biopsy must be balanced against
diagnostic and therapeutic yield. A meta-analysis of over 118,000 native kidney biopsies
reported low rates of significant complications, such as major bleeding (0.3%) and death
(0.06%).272 A multicenter, ICU-based study that included biopsies of 68 native kidneys (87%
using ultrasound guidance), showed that a specific diagnosis other than ATN was established
in 35 (51%) critically ill participants, and altered management decisions in 14 (21%)
participants.273 Biopsy complications occurred in 22% of participants of whom only 2 were
111

graded as severe (requirement for kidney embolization). Although major complications after
native kidney biopsy are uncommon in the general inpatient and outpatient populations,
complication rates are higher in people who are critically ill and those undergoing biopsy in
the ICU, where hemodynamic instability, coagulopathy, and greater illness severity are more
frequent.

resistive index (RRI), may aid in risk stratification for AKI progression and severity,

not be interpreted as a measure of intrinsic renal vascular resistance alone. Thus, Doppler
ultrasound measurements of RRI (and optionally RPI) may be used as adjuncts for early
prediction of AKI, alongside standard clinical and laboratory parameters.

Practice Point 2.5.9: In hospitalized or critically ill patients with AKI, point-of-care
ultrasound (POCUS) may assist in assessing volume status and distinguishing volume
responsive AKI from intrinsic AKI.
Ultrasound is a readily available, noninvasive test that can help identify potentially
reversible causes of AKI. In selected people presenting with AKI who have risk factors,
112

signs, or symptoms of urinary tract obstruction (e.g., anuria/oliguria, lower urinary tract
symptoms, known or suspected stones, pelvic malignancy, benign prostatic hyperplasia,
neurogenic bladder, prior urologic surgery, or an indwelling catheter with poor output),
kidney and bladder ultrasound (KUB) should be considered to evaluate for hydronephrosis
and bladder outlet obstruction, as these findings may prompt immediate, kidney-sparing
interventions. In addition to excluding obstruction, bedside ultrasound can be integrated with
point-of-care assessments to support hemodynamic evaluation in hospitalized and critically
ill patients with AKI.
T
Clinical differentiation between volume-responsive and intrinsic AKI (with structural
kidney damage) can be a diagnostic challenge, particularly in people with conditions that
make clinical volume assessment difficult. POCUS has emerged as a valuable adjunct to
physical examination, offering real-time, noninvasive insights into intravascular volume,
F
venous congestion, and structural abnormalities.
Multiorgan POCUS assessing cardiac, lung, abdominal, and venous structures can aid
AKI management by identificationA of volume overload, venous congestion, and cardiac
dysfunction. The inferior vena cava collapsibility index (IVC-CI) is a particularly useful
POCUS-derived parameter for assessing fluid responsiveness and identifying people at risk
for hypotension. A more detailed framework for AKI classification using POCUS has been
proposed by Varanasi et al., where hemodynamic AKI is assessed using IVC collapsibility,
R
lung B-lines, and echocardiographic surrogates.280 Obstructive causes such as hydronephrosis
and bladder outlet obstruction can also be readily identified at the bedside.
POCUS has been increasingly incorporated into management of AKI. In people who
D
are critically ill with cirrhosis and hepatorenal syndrome, POCUS has been described to
assess fluid status, cardiac function, and venous congestion, thereby influencing decisions
around albumin therapy and vasopressor use.281 Operator dependency is the major limitation.
Multiorgan POCUS is most informative when performed using a standardized
protocol to assess volume status, venous congestion, and fluid responsiveness, and when
supported by structured training, competency assessment, and ongoing quality assurance.

Values and preferences
This recommendation places a high value on early identification of risk of severe AKI
and need for RRT in critically ill children. Patients, families, and healthcare professionals
generally prioritize strategies that reduce the risk of organ failure, invasive therapies,
prolonged hospitalization, and long-term kidney disease, even when supported by low-
certainty evidence, provided that the intervention is low risk. The moderate-to-high
sensitivity of the RAI aligns with preferences to avoid unnecessary investigations or
interventions in children at low risk, while focusing attention and resources on those most
T
likely to benefit from closer monitoring or kidney-protective strategies.
Families may value the use of a transparent risk assessment tool that facilitates
anticipatory guidance, earlier communication about potential kidney-related complications,
F
and proactive care planning, including preparedness for possible RRT. At the same time,
some families and healthcare professionals may place greater weight on avoiding over-
monitoring or anxiety related to false-positive risk classification, particularly in the absence
of definitive evidence that RAI-guiAded interventions improve outcomes. Thus, there is likely
variability in how patients, families, and healthcare professionals weigh the benefits of early
risk identification against the potential for increased monitoring and resource use. However,
given the noninvasive nature of the RAI and the serious outcomes it seeks to prevent, most
stakeholders would favor its use in children at risk for AKI, supporting a Level 2 statement.
R
Resource use and costs
Use of the RAI incurs minimal direct resource use as it is calculated using clinical
variables that are routinely collected in standard inpatient care. Calculating and interpreting
the RDAI requires modest healthcare professional time, which may be integrated into routine
assessments by physicians, nurses, or clinical support staff. Some initial investment in
training healthcare professionals and staff on how to calculate, interpret, and act on the RAI
may be necessary.
Use of the RAI may lead to increased monitoring, additional nursing assessments, or
increased consultation for children identified as high risk. These downstream activities may
incur additional costs, though they may also prevent costly complications through
appropriate early intervention. Overall, the resource use and costs associated with the RAI
are relatively low compared with the potential clinical benefits of identifying children at high
risk for severe AKI and RRT.
Considerations for implementation
The RAI is most effectively applied early during hospitalization, particularly within
the first 12–24 hours of admission or onset of critical illness. Clear identification of target
populations (i.e., critically ill children) is important to avoid unnecessary application in
people at low risk.

Integration of the RAI into routine clinical workflows can enhance feasibility and
consistency. Where available, embedding automated RAI calculation and alerts within the
EHR may reduce healthcare professional burden and improve uptake. In settings without
electronic automation, standardized paper or electronic checklists can be used with minimal
additional resources. Education and training of physicians, nurses, and allied health
professionals are essential to ensure correct calculation, interpretation, and appropriate
clinical response to RAI results.
